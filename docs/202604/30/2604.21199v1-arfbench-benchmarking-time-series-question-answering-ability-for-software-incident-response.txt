## Abstract 

Time series question-answering (TSQA), in which we ask natural language questions to infer and reason about properties of time series, is a promising yet underexplored capability of foundation models. In this work, we present ARFBench , a TSQA benchmark that evaluates the under-standing of multimodal foundation models (FMs) on time series anomalies prevalent in software incident data. ARFBench consists of 750 questions across 142 time series and 5.38M data points from 63 production incidents sourced exclusively from internal telemetry at Datadog. We evaluate leading proprietary and open-source LLMs, VLMs, and time series FMs and observe that frontier VLMs perform markedly better than existing baselines; the leading model (GPT-5) achieves a 62.7% accuracy and 51.9% F1. We next demonstrate the promise of specialized multimodal approaches. We develop a novel TSFM + VLM hybrid prototype which we post-train on a small set of synthetic and real data that yields comparable overall F1 and accuracy with frontier models. Lastly, we find models and human domain experts exhibit complementary strengths. We define a model-expert oracle, a best-of-2 oracle selector over model and expert answers, yielding 82.8% F1 and 87.2% accuracy and establishing a new superhuman frontier for future TSQA models. The benchmark is available at https://huggingface.co/datasets/Datadog/ARFBench. 

## 1 Introduction 

Trillions of dollars are lost each year due to system outages (Krasner, 2018). In order to resolve unexpected software incidents in a timely manner, engineers must decide on their next actions and determine where errors occur. To guide these choices, engineers rely on observability metrics , or time series used to monitor the health of production software, such as CPU usage, latency, and error counts, among others. Engineers investigate incidents by repeatedly asking questions about these time series, such as “Is the pattern in this time series abnormal?” , or “Are these two time series correlated?” . These questions naturally give rise to time series question answering (TSQA) tasks, in which reasoning over multiple time series and temporal patterns is necessary in order to support downstream decision-making. Despite the central role of such question-driven analysis in incident response, it remains unclear whether modern foundation models can reliably answer the kinds of time series questions engineers ask in practice. To this end, we present the Anomaly Reasoning Framework Bench mark ( ARFBench ), a multimodal, multiple-choice question-answering benchmark designed to test time series reasoning and understanding tasks that are constructed from real software incidents sourced exclusively from internal telemetry at Datadog, a leading observability platform vendor, with sensitive data anonymized by both an LLM and manual review. ARFBench is distinguished by three key aspects: (i) it is grounded in real production time series from operational systems, (ii) each QA pair is supported by expert annotations and additional incident context, and (iii) tasks are designed to evaluate compositional anomaly reasoning, with questions organized into three tiers of increasing difficulty, with higher-tier tasks depending on correct reasoning over lower tiers. ARFBench questions are designed in tiers that capture increasing levels of reasoning: first detecting anomalies, then identifying properties of anomalies, and lastly reasoning about the relationships between anomalies across time series. These questions test general time series understanding abilities such as event classification, magnitude estimation, trend and periodicity analysis, and also require robust understanding of correlation and temporal relationships (Section 3.1). 1

## 2 Related Work 

Evaluating Multimodal Time Series Reasoning and QA. Existing works explore multimodal time series understanding through classical tasks such as forecasting Williams et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024); Tu et al. (2022) or in chart analysis Masry et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2023); Shen et al. (2025). Recently, TSQA benchmarks have emerged as a test of reasoning ability. Synthetic TSQA benchmarks Merrill et al. (2024); Cai et al. (2024a); Anonymous (2026); Wang et al. (2025b); Divo et al. (2025); Xie et al. (2024), where the time series is synthetic, have been a popular benchmarking approach. Real-world TSQA benchmarks are less common and often are derived from pre-existing benchmarks or datasets: ECG-QA Oh et al. (2023), TS-Insights Zhang et al. (2023), Time-mqa Kong et al. (2025), TimerBed Liu et al. (2025), and TSR-Suite Guan et al. (2025) generate QA pairs over diverse domains using publicly available time series data, but lack reasoning complexity in multivariate or multi-time series analysis and often use synthetic anomaly injection. Furthermore, many real-world TSQA datasets derive synthetic question-answer pairs without contextual evidence, i.e. there are no auxiliary human annotations that act as primary evidence for the proposed labels. Among existing benchmarks, TelecomTS Feng et al. (2025) and Time-RA Yang et al. (2025) are the most similar to this work, but neither benchmark evaluates on reasoning over multiple time series, nor are they grounded in expert annotations. Moreover, existing benchmarks often evaluate in a single-task framework, training separate models for each task. In contrast, ARFBench tests TSQA ability over eight different anomaly-related reasoning tasks with observability time series derived from real software incidents. Enriched with context and human annotations, the time series and QA pairs in ARFBench present a novel challenge in TSQA and reasoning, grounded in real-world and complex data. 

Time Series Anomaly Detection. Time series anomaly detection is the task of proactively predicting or reactively finding significant deviations from expected behavior and trends in time series Chandola et al. (2009); Li et al. (2025); Xu et al. (2024); Goswami et al. (2022). Many benchmarks, including those grounded in observability, have been developed to assess anomaly detection Lavin and Ahmad (2015); Ahmad et al. (2017); Han et al. (2022); Schmidl et al. (2022); Si et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2025). Our work is related to, but fundamentally distinct from, classical anomaly detection benchmarks. Traditional anomaly detection formulations typically require models to output precise anomalous time ranges, and their evaluation relies on specialized metrics that attempt to reconcile ambiguity in anomaly boundaries and downstream utility (Huet et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2021; Paparrizos et al., 2022; Liu and Paparrizos, 2024). These benchmarks also commonly exhibit disagreement in ground-truth labeling, reflecting the inherent subjectivity in defining exact anomaly start and end times (Wu and Keogh, 2021). In contrast, ARFBench does not evaluate anomaly detection as a time-range prediction problem. Instead, we focus on high-level anomaly reasoning by framing incident analysis as a series of multiple-choice questions. Specifically, we split the task into three separate single-class classification tasks (presence, start time, end time). In doing so, we penalize performance for incorrect answers equally and thus allow a set of time ranges to equally represent the valid time range of the anomaly; the model need only find the answer that is closest in time to the ground truth. This simplified task measures reasoning relevant to downstream decisions in incident response, where exactness of time ranges is usually not necessary. Beyond classical anomaly detection, ARFBench also includes tasks that require reasoning about anomaly types and relationships between 3Figure 2: Example questions in ARFBench for each tier. For each question, the model is given a time series caption along with corresponding time series data in image or tabular form. In addition to the input above, time series channels are named with tags that represent collections such as datacenters, pods, services, and more. multiple time series, such as anomaly categorization and cross-metric anomaly correlation. 

Modeling Methods in TSQA. Recent works have studied how zero-shot VLMs and reinforcement learning (RL) post-trained VLMs Chow et al. (2024); Liang et al. (2025); Daswani et al. (2024); Sen et al. (2025); Liu et al. (2025); Zhang et al. (2025b); He et al. (2025) perform on time series tasks such as time series classification, TSQA, and captioning on existing time series data. VLMs have also been used for understanding anomalies in synthetic time series data Xie et al. (2024); Zhou and Yu (2024); Shen et al. (2022, 2024) as well as representation and reasoning in TSFMs Potosnak et al. (2025); Wili´ nski et al. (2024); Yu et al. (2025a). In parallel, multimodal time series LLM and VLM models have received increasing attention for forecasting Zhong et al. (2025); Zhang et al. (2025a); Kim et al. (2024); Cheng et al. (2022), anomaly detection and classification Zhang et al. (2024); Ebrahimi et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2025c); Lan et al. (2025), and summarization Cai et al. (2024b); Zhang et al. (2025c). Unlike existing methods that train time series encoders from scratch, we utilize a novel approach to combine and train pretrained TSFMs with VLMs jointly, demonstrating promise in multimodal foundation model approaches to TSQA modeling. ARFBench is designed to allow evaluation of reasoning ability using vision, language, and time series data modalities within a well-structured TSQA framework. 

## 3 ARFBench 

In this section, we describe the problem and benchmark design, data sources, and data generation pipeline for ARFBench .

3.1 Problem Definition 

Each question in ARFBench consists of a templated question prompt, a caption describing one or two univariate or multivariate time series of interest, and the associated time series data. In each time series, there may be zero, one, or multiple anomalies. The 8 question categories are designed to test escalating levels of understanding (Figure 1). By design, as the difficulty of the question increases, the questions require more complex reasoning and additional multimodal evidence. We assign each category to a tier under this framework. The Tier I difficulty (Presence ) consists of binary decisions that identify the presence or absence of an anomaly. The Tier II difficulty ( Identification, Magnitude, Start Time, End Time, Categorization ) corresponds to questions that require analysis of a single (possibly multivariate) time series in order to characterize specific properties of an anomaly or identify anomalous channels, thus necessitating reasoning about the time range and series values. Tier III difficulty ( Correlation, Leading/Lagging Indicator ) targets paired time series reasoning. In addition to Tier I and II-style detection and localization within each series, the model must compare anomaly structure across series—including temporal alignment and relative ordering—to 4Figure 3: ARFBench requires multivariate reasoning. A single time series variate taken out of context may not appear to be anomalous. However, in a structured multivariate grouping, the variate would be considered anomalous. More details about question design can be found in Appendix B.1. 

3.2 Key Design Decisions Defining Anomaly Understanding as a Classification Problem. The questions in ARFBench 

all focus on understanding time series anomalies, and therefore it is important for us to define anomalies in a consistent manner. Historically, anomalies have been defined around scoring either points or subsequences of the time series with anomaly scores Chandola et al. (2009). Evaluation of anomaly detection has proven to be difficult, both from the benchmark and methodological side, since ambiguities exist both in terms of the ground truth (e.g. where should the start of an anomaly be?) and the evaluation metrics (e.g. how should one penalize the start of a predicted anomaly subsequence based on its distance from the start of the ground truth?). Improving evaluations in anomaly detection is still an active area of research (see Section 2 for more details). Here, we choose to sidestep issues with ground truth determination and choice of measure by using a multiple-choice format. In doing so, we eliminate boundary problems by constructing answer choices with a single correct choice relative to the rest. 

Incorporating Additional Context is Necessary For Correct Reasoning. Anomalies are inherently contextual: whether a pattern is considered anomalous depends not only on the observed values, but also on temporal context, the semantic meaning of the signal, and its relationships to other time series. As additional context becomes available—such as longer historical windows or richer metadata about what a signal represents—the interpretation of anomalous behavior can fundamentally change. A small spike in the time series that measures unauthorized access attempts for a service may be much more severe than a brief spike in the latency of the same service. The former could suggest compromised systems, while the latter may be transient noise, with little long-term impact. Therefore, an important component of detecting and analyzing anomalies is understanding what the time series represents. In ARFBench , we include this crucial context by summarizing the queries used to fetch individual time series examples from a database (Figure 5, Appendix). Moreover, multivariate groupings (Figure 3) provide insight into whether a time series variate is anomalous or not. Although multivariate anomaly detection could be done by analyzing each variate individually, this misses the broader relationship of the variate to the multivariate grouping, if structure exists. For example, a service with one pod failing and restarting may not be significant, but a combination of pods in the same service failing and restarting simultaneously may be a critical error. In ARFBench , all time series variates are grouped under meaningful structure, e.g. variates are grouped across specific services, datacenters, or other attributes. This requires TSQA models to reason about anomalies in a multivariate rather than univariate fashion. 53.3 Software Incident Timelines as Expert Annotations Software Incidents. Formally, software incidents are unplanned degradations or failures of a software system that deviate from its specified or expected behavior. Diagnosing such incidents is inherently complex, as it requires engineers to interpret noisy telemetry signals, form hypotheses, and iteratively refine their understanding of the system under evolving context. 

Software Incident Timelines. Our primary source of data comes from software incident timelines. These timelines document the real-time process of incident investigation and resolution, including Slack Slack Technologies, LLC (2009) discussion threads between engineers at Datadog that begin when a software incident is detected and continue throughout the mitigation process. These timelines provide a real-time record of expert reasoning, including the questions engineers ask, the evidence they examine, and the conclusions they draw over time. Interspersed with their natural language discussions, these engineers can also share embedded data visualization widgets in order to help triage and diagnose degraded systems. These visualizations encompass a wide range of telemetry data modalities, including logs, traces, and metrics. For ARFBench ,we focus on metrics — time series data that are a key tool for anomaly reasoning and cross-metric analysis in investigations. 

3.4 QA Generation Pipeline 

Our data collection process primarily involves three steps: data curation from real incidents, QA generation, and filtering and verification. 

“Oracle” Model. In the construction of ARFbench , the discussion data is crucially used as extra context along with the time series plot as input to an “oracle” VLM to generate choices and select putative labels for questions, akin to an offline version of how expert annotations are used to label anomalies in Si et al. (2024). For particular question types, the incident timeline provides messages supporting exact answers within the text. Furthermore, having access to the incident timeline allows better understanding of the time series in comparison to other time series from the same incident. This allows for a zero-shot baseline that allows the model to “cheat”. We use the oracle VLM for the generation, filtering, and verification steps because it allows for automated, scalable question generation when analyzing complex incident data. The incident data often requires expert domain knowledge in specific software systems or infrastructure to understand, and having the VLM synthesize such information immensely speeds up this process to generate feasible answer choices. The answers are then verified by humans by using the incident report data, which allows us to prevent leakage of sensitive data and filter the questions for erroneous answer choices. 

Data Curation and Cleaning From Incidents. From each incident, we extract one or more time series to be used in QA generation. Each time series has an associated query string that maps to the given time series in a large-scale database. To remove sensitive information, we use an LLM to summarize and sanitize this query data, and we provide it as the time series caption for additional context at inference time. In addition, we include tag names corresponding to the different variates of the time series, giving context to what each channel represents (e.g. datacenter, asset, error type). See Section A.1 for further details on how the time series caption is generated. 

Answer Choice and Putative Label Generation. For each time series, we generate questions using a standardized set of templates, filling in relevant time series information such as the title and channel names. Following question generation, the question, time series plot, and full incident discussion data are used as context to prompt (App. G.2) the oracle VLM to generate answer choices relevant to the particular time series, as well as to choose a putative answer choice. Some question categories (i.e. Presence, Correlation, Indicator) have a fixed set of answer choices, with no VLM generation involved. For all other categories, the VLM generates anywhere between five and eight possible answer choices. 

Filtering and Verification. After generation, the questions are filtered by an LLM to ensure sensibility and relevance of the question-answer pairs, since some question types are more suitable for some time series than others, e.g. the question category is directly mentioned in the discussion of the incident timeline. Finally, the authors manually filtered and corrected all remaining QA pairs to ensure accuracy and no leakage of sensitive data. ## 4 Experimental Results 

4.1 Evaluation Metrics 

To evaluate each model, we compute overall, per-tier, and per-category accuracy and multiclass macro-F1. Multiclass macro-F1 is the unweighted mean of the F1 score computed separately for each class, treating each class in turn as the positive label and all others as negative. Accuracy and multiclass macro-F1 serve complementary roles as evaluation metrics. Accuracy measures overall task correctness and provides an interpretable measure of benchmark-level success, while macro-F1 measures whether that performance is balanced across answer classes rather than driven by a small subset of frequent labels. 

Macro-F1 prevents exploitation of accuracy. The F1 score is used in conjunction with accuracy to account for the label imbalance inherent in incident data due to the high prevalence of anomalies. Baselines such as Frequent Choice, which always answer using the most common class, can yield deceptively high accuracy on some categories (e.g., Presence) while producing uninformative predictions. Multiclass macro-F1 explicitly penalizes such predictors, achieving at most 1# classes .For each question, answer choices may be data-dependent (e.g. Magnitude: 1, 10, 100, 1000), so the raw answer choices are not comparable across questions. Instead, we map raw answer choices to predefined sets of semantic answer classes for each category in a one-to-one mapping (e.g. Smallest, Small, Medium, Large, No Anomaly). The F1 score is computed across the semantic answer choices to be comparable across classes. The semantic class definitions are given in the Appendix B.2. We find that our per-category frequent choice baseline, which chooses the most frequent label in each category (and selects randomly if the most frequent label does not exist in the answer choices), yields the second lowest F1 of all models evaluated at 17.3%. 

Accuracy complements macro-F1. Macro-F1 is useful for preventing models from exploiting label imbalance, but it does not fully reflect performance on ARFBench. Since it weights all answer classes equally, macro-F1 can be sensitive to performance on rare classes and is less directly interpretable than accuracy. 

4.2 Baselines and Model Details Models. We use a few-shot prompting method (Appendix G.4) for all models, regardless of post-training. We refer models without post-training as “few shot”. Among few-shot models, we evaluate 9 state-of-the-art open-source and proprietary VLMs on our benchmark, as well as 2 open-source and proprietary LLMs. Furthermore, we evaluate 2 existing post-trained foundation models, both of which are time series LLM models. For each model, we generally choose the newest available checkpoint in order to test the variant with the top expected reasoning capabilities. As a simple baseline, we evaluate Random Choice as well as per-category Frequent Choice, which picks the most frequently correct answer per category if it exists in the answer choices, otherwise a randomly selected valid answer is chosen. 7User Study. Users were given a 16-question calibration set selected from the post-training dataset, which they were required to obtain at least 90% accuracy to move on to the full user study.

4.3 Training Multimodal TSQA Models Motivation. Time series in ARFBench are difficult to represent as input for few-shot LLMs and VLMs due to high variate count and length (Table 2). Furthermore, we find that existing few-shot models have major areas for improvement (Section 4). This motivates the need for specialized time series foundation models. As an initial attempt in modeling, we post-trained three model families: VLMs, TSFM-LLMs, and TSFM-VLMs. Further details on post-training can be found in Appendix D.

Synthetic Training Data. To post-train all models, we first generated 12,000 synthetic training examples by starting with random Gaussian noise and then injecting predefined anomalies (Figure 7). The anomalies are injected uniformly at random into locations in the time range. Furthermore, the anomaly magnitude is scaled by a randomly chosen factor of the maximum value of the time series. 

Real Training Data. We also generated and manually labeled an additional set of 207 training examples, using the same pipeline as the main benchmark, but over a date range temporally disjoint and drawn from distinct incidents than the set of incidents used for ARFBench . The real labeled dataset was then augmented to 395 examples with negative tier III augmentation (Appendix A.5). 

TSFM-VLM Architecture. Training time series-vision-language models has only been recently explored for time series forecasting Zhong et al. (2025). Such approaches rely on training from scratch. By contrast, recent work has shown promise in using pretrained TSFMs for time series classification Auer et al. (2025). Since ARFBench utilizes observability metrics, we jointly train a leading observability forecasting TSFM, Toto Cohen et al. (2025), and a reasonably-sized leading open-source VLM, Qwen3-VL 32B Bai et al. (2025) (Figure 4). To adapt Toto and Qwen3-VL to work together, we train two components to transform embeddings from Toto into the VLM embedding space. First, the variate embedding multi-layer perceptron (MLP) aggregates embeddings across the time dimension, then normalizes and projects the resulting embeddings through a simple MLP. Importantly, the variate embedding MLP prevents the textual context length from scaling with the length of the series. The VLM projection layers then project the embeddings to the hidden dimension of the text decoder. 

Training Pipeline. For both the TSFM-LLM and TSFM-VLM models, we used a three-stage training approach. The models were first trained with supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on the synthetic data (Stage 1), then with SFT on the real data (Stage 2). Stage 3 continues training from the Stage 2 checkpoint using RLVR with the DAPO recipe Yu et al. (2025b), a PPO-style asymmetric clipped objective. For the post-trained Qwen3-VL (without Toto), we omit the SFT phases and use only the RL stage, as this empirically led to the best results. For each stage, we trained a different LoRA Hu et al. (2022) adapter for the text decoder and applied it sequentially before training the next stage. Early stopping was done for the SFT stages based on validation loss. We freeze prior-stage adapters when training subsequent stages; at evaluation we compose all adapters sequentially. 4.4 Results 

We present overall and per-tier accuracy and F1 scores for all models in Table 3. Per-category results can be found in Table 7 and 8 in the Appendix. 

Frontier VLMs Substantially Lead Over Existing Baselines. The top few-shot model on ARF-Bench , GPT-5, yielded a 62.7% accuracy and 51.9% F1. This improves significantly over random choice, at 24.5% accuracy (22.5% F1), as well as over other open-source and proprietary baselines outside the GPT-5 family by at least 4.6 percentage points (pp) accuracy and 2.3 pp F1. As expected, the models perform worse as the difficulty of the question increases. Tier III was the most difficult tier for all models to achieve high F1, with the best model achieving 48.4% F1. This trend tends to persist in accuracy, though the top models tend to have higher accuracy in Tier III than Tier II. However, this can be contextualized by per-category frequent choice, showing that the label imbalance is greater in Tier III than in Tier II. We also corroborate several previously observed trends in TSQA. First, for both LLMs we evaluated, the overall and tier-wise performance lagged behind the VLM counterpart of the same model, aligning with results from Daswani et al. (2024). Furthermore, while most VLMs are relatively strong at identifying the presence of an anomaly, they still struggle with reliably determining properties of the anomaly (Tier II) relative to humans, with a 7.3 pp accuracy and 13.3 pp F1 difference between non-domain experts and GPT-5. This matches the conclusions of Zhou and Yu (2024), particularly that visual perception in vision-language models does not match human perception of anomalies. The top proprietary and open-source few-shot VLM models also showed a substantial gap in performance, with the best open-source model (Qwen3-VL 32B) performing 9.9 pp and 6.8 pp F1 worse overall compared to the best proprietary model (GPT-5). The main difference in performance between the two categories of models comes from Tiers II and III, where the model must reliably extract details from time series to answer the question. However, we find that some open-source models perform better than many older proprietary models or models from the Claude family.

While these approaches may improve significantly over few-shot LLM-only inference of the base model, time series LLMs still lag significantly behind VLMs of similar size. Model and Expert Performance Show Complementarity. Given the 8.8 pp accuracy and 12.7 pp F1 gap in performance between the top model and expert performance, we wanted to understand whether models and users had largely similar errors, which would imply difficult or unanswerable questions. To compare, we took the results from the two top models by accuracy (GPT-5 and Toto-1.0-QA-Experimental) and the two domain experts across the sampled 25% of questions used for the user study. We found that among questions that both experts got incorrect (23 questions), GPT-5 and Toto-1.0-QA-Experimental individually got 47.8% (11 questions) and 34% (8 questions) correct, respectively. Conversely, we find that among the questions that GPT-5 and Toto-1.0-QA-Experimental individually answer incorrectly (58 questions and 59 questions, respectively), at least one expert correctly answers the same questions 79.3% (46 questions) and 74.6% (44 questions) of the time. Therefore, given a large distributional mismatch between the questions models and humans answer correctly, we find evidence against unanswerable questions and an expert-level performance ceiling. Furthermore, we find that the errors made between models and experts are qualitatively different: experts tend to make fine-grained perceptual errors and instruction-following errors that may result from the lack of interactivity of the interface and tediousness of the task, while models tend to make domain-knowledge errors and hallucinate inconsistent answers at times. We present case studies in Appendix F.2. To establish a baseline on the performance of experts when augmented with model predictions, we compute a best-of-2 score, where we assume a perfect judge that picks a correct answer whenever presented with two plausible choices. Using the results from GPT-5 and a domain expert, we hypothesize that this ”Model-Expert Oracle” achieves 87.2% accuracy and 82.8% F1, demonstrating that under ideal conditions, experts augmented with model predictions perform far better than models or humans alone. 10 4.5 Ablations Ablating Training Stages of Toto-1.0-QA-Experimental. As discussed in Section 4.3, Toto-1.0-QA-Experimental is trained in three separate stages: an SFT stage with synthetic data, an SFT stage with real data, and a real data RLVR stage. We find that the performance at each stage varies heavily. The performance after the initial SFT stage is poor: a near 0 score is obtained across most questions. Since the model generates reasoning blocks without a final answer, it yields no score for a majority of questions. After the second SFT stage, Toto-1.0-QA-Experimental yields a 48.4% accuracy and 37.8% F1, which is significantly lower than the base Qwen3-VL 32B model performance (52.8% accuracy and 45.1% F1). However, this stage has relatively few answer formatting issues, suggesting that the VLM has adapted to utilizing the time series tokens. The final RLVR stage refines the model’s ability to use time series embeddings and raises the accuracy by 15.4 pp (11 pp F1) from the second SFT stage. By contrast, we also find that the SFT stages play an important role in the joint adaptation of the TSFM and VLM. By omitting one or both SFT stages, we found that the RL training was significantly slower to improve in the first 100 steps, achieving a per-step mean reward that was around 2-4 times less than the per-step mean reward after the two SFT stages. We leave the question of understanding the role of the SFT stages in RL as future work to understanding training dynamics for TSFM-VLM models. 

VLM Context Ablations. We experiment with context ablations to test the significance of each component of the multimodal input context in model performance (Table 4). To do this, we test GPT-4o and GPT-5 on ARFBench when there are (1) no provided plots of the time series (2) no time series descriptions (captions) to condition on what the time series represents. We find that, for both models, when no time series data is provided (in image or text format), the model performs between random choice and per-category frequent choice in overall and per-tier F1 as well as accuracy. When only the time series data is provided but not the caption, GPT-5 performs substantially better (+23.4 pp accuracy / +24.2 pp F1) than GPT-5 with no time series data, but still fairly below GPT-5 with full input. By contrast, we find that GPT-4o performs better in accuracy when no caption is provided, though overall F1 still remains worse than having full input. Thus, additional context is not uniformly utilized across models in ARFBench. 

## 5 Limitations and Future Work 

ARFBench presents a novel benchmark for evaluating the capabilities of multimodal models and introduces new modeling approaches to TSQA. However, ARFBench leaves out more open-ended questions commonly asked in the incident response process, such as common mitigation strategies (e.g. 

Based on this anomaly, should we scale up resources? ) or linking to common incident patterns (e.g. Is this anomaly directly linked to a deployment? ), which may require further labeled data. In addition, 

ARFBench is still limited to single-turn QA, which means it is not yet a comprehensive benchmark for agentic tasks or QA. On the modeling end, there is more work to understand why TSFM-VLM models perform so well on certain tasks and how to improve such models. We leave such tasks as future work. 

## 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we present ARFBench , a TSQA benchmark designed to test reasoning capabilities grounded in the software observability domain. We demonstrate that novel joint time series and vision-language modeling shows great promise, and we hope that the open-source release of ARFBench will be a valuable resource for the ML community to build upon existing methods for time series and software incident response.

All questions from the benchmark are generated from 63 incidents resolved on March 7th, 2025 to March 30th, 2025. Each time series within each incident is extracted, the channel names are anonymized, and a plot is generated for the time series. Due to computational overhead, we limit the time series we extract to under 2500 non-empty variates. For each time series, all single time series question templates are applied to generate a candidate question. For each incident, we select random pairs of time series (up to 10 pairs) and apply all paired time series question templates. For each question, we also prompt an LLM to select a putative answer. Then, all questions are filtered according to our filtering prompt, which aims to remove questions that clearly do not have overlaps in time range. The remaining questions are then uploaded to the open source labeling platform Label Studio, where the authors reviewed and changed the putative answers if necessary. 

A.1 Time Series Caption 

The time series description or caption comes from summarizing database queries used to fetch the corresponding time series (Figure 5). This database query language utilizes space aggregations, time aggregations, filters, and more to select particular time series variates from a large database. In the construction of ARFBench , we only collect query strings from internal users and systems and do not use any customer data. These query strings are then summarized with an LLM to anonymize sensitive information and used as context in each question, as explained in Section 3.4.

A.2 Software Incident Timelines In Context of Incident Response 

As mentioned, software incident timelines provide a source of expert annotations to help label our benchmark. Connecting to broader incident response workflow, the incident timeline logs the overall process of incident response, which typically consists of (but is not limited to) following nonlinear steps: 1. Incident Report: Alerting or triggering the beginning of the incident. 2. Diagnosis: Understanding the severity, systems, and customers that are affected by the incident. 3. Triage: Prioritizing the workflow to target different impacted components based on severity and other factors. 4. Mitigation: Working towards changes that reduce the negative effects of the incident. 5. Resolution: Finding solutions that resolve the downstream impacts of the incident. 6. Root Cause Analysis (RCA): Finding the specific cause of the incident. 7. Postmortem: Creating a write-up of what lessons were learned from the incident. TSQA and reasoning can be a crucial tool in many of these steps, especially in triage, mitigation, and RCA. For example, looking at a time series of error rates can help to triage by identifying the portion of traffic affected. Comparing multiple time series can help identify which system component is the source of errors, aiding in RCA. Moreover, after a mitigation is attempted, responders may want to assess whether anomalous behavior in a time series has resolved to a normal state. 17 Figure 6: Distribution of question categories (left) and time series domains (right) in ARFBench. 

A.3 Observability Time Series Subdomains 

Using the captions of each time series, we classify each time series into one or more of various subdomains of observability (Figure 6). These include: 1. Application Usage : data collected from the application layer within a service 2. Infrastructure : metrics of common system measurements, such as CPU usage or GPU utilization 3. Networking : metrics of network- and communication-related measurements, e.g. latency 4. Database : data collected when performing database operations, such as querying 5. Security : metrics that monitor the general security of a system, such as vulnerability messages Including each of these domains ensures a diverse benchmark with time series of diverse characteristics. To categorize the domains of the time series in ARFBench we use GPT-4.1 to classify each query string. 

A.4 Answer Choice Filtering 

During the benchmark construction phase, the LLM pipeline will potentially generate more answer choices than are used in the final QA pair, depending on the question category. This is reflected in the prompts. We postprocess the benchmark to filter down to five answer choices per category, following the semantic answer classes given in Appendix B. 

A.5 Negative Tier III Augmentation 

During question generation, questions in Tier III are generated between pairs of time series from the same incident. This often results in correlated time series, leading to a biased label distribution that may be unrepresentative of the task at hand in the real-world (i.e. the pairs of time series may not always be hand-selected by experts when a model is asked to compare them). In order to smooth the biased label distribution in Tier III after the data pipeline, we augment Tier III of the benchmark with questions between time series that are not in the same incident, which we determine to not be correlated or indicative of each other.

A time series is not limited to one anomaly, however, the question is designed to test for at least one anomaly. The answer choices for the presence question are in binary Yes/No format. 

Identification. The Identification question tests whether a model can distinguish which subset of channels within the given three channels within a time series are anomalous. The given channels are chosen randomly. In total, there are five answer choices, two answer choices of single variate answer choices, one pair of variates, and the triplet of all three variates in question. 

Start Time. The Start Time question tests whether a model can find the time stamp at which an anomaly begins. An anomaly may not exist within the time series. Within a given time series, the start time refers to the beginning of the first anomaly of the time series. The anomaly may begin before the start of the time series data and this case should be jointly inferred from the time series description and data. Answer choices correspond to different time stamps along the time series, with one answer as Before the earliest time stamp of the given options. 

End Time. The End Time question tests whether a model can find the time stamp at which an anomaly ends. Within a given time series, the end time refers to the end of the last anomaly of the time series. The anomaly may end after the end of the time series data and this case should be jointly inferred from the time series description and data. Answer choices correspond to different time stamps along the time series, which are not guaranteed to lie within the time range of the time series, with one answer as Not resolved referring to the end time of the anomaly being after the end of the given time series data. 

Magnitude. The Magnitude question tests whether a model can correctly assess the deviation of the time series from an expected counterfactual forecast. In general, this is calculated as the maximum ratio between the anomalous values and the mean counterfactual values. In the case that the counterfactual values are zero, the magnitude is instead calculated as the maximum absolute value of the anomalous values. For this question, the LLM is prompted to generate reasonable answer choices in a logarithmic scale. 19 Categorization. The Categorization question tests whether a model can correctly classify an anomaly into one of five anomaly categories relevant for software incident response, which include: level shift, transient spike, change in seasonality, change in variance, and change in trend. We qualitatively describe these categories in relation to the traditional categories of anomalies in anomaly detection Chandola et al. (2009) (i.e. point, contextual, and collective anomalies), but we find that the granularity of the definitions for anomaly detection is not sufficient for analyzing anomalies relevant in software incident response. A level shift represents a prolonged change in mean value of the time series. The level shift may arise as a contextual anomaly, where the mean value is substantially different than what is expected from the general value of the time series in relation to its time series description, or it may be a collective anomaly, in which we see an identifiable subsequence of the data have a change in mean value over time. A transient spike is a type of point anomaly, where a small collection of points are substantially different in magnitude than other points in the time series. Transient spikes often return to the original values and are very short in duration, leading to little to no shift in mean value. A change in seasonality is a type of contextual or collective anomaly, where the magnitude of the time series does not change, but the seasonal pattern of the time series changes in frequency. A change in variance describes when the variance of the time series significantly shifts from its previous value for a sustained period of time. A change in trend is a contextual or collective anomaly in which the time series increases or decreases for a significant period of time. Multiple such anomalies may occur in one series, but in general, we consider the one with the largest magnitude or most relevant to the incident timeline to be the correct answer. 

Correlation. The Correlation question tests whether a model correctly identifies correlations between two time series as well as a model can correctly identify the existence of anomalies in both. Two anomalies are correlated if they have temporally similar patterns in context to the time series descriptions, or if they have known relation through the software infrastructure based on what they represent. 

Indicator. The Indicator question is a follow-up question to the correlation question, testing a model on whether it can identify the temporal order between anomalies. The answer choices include that the first anomaly is a leading indicator of the second anomaly, a lagging indicator of the second anomaly, perfectly correlated between the two anomalies, or not correlated at all. 

B.2 Semantic Binning Categories Presence. The semantic classes of Presence are the same as the answer choice classes (i.e. Yes/No). 

Identification. The semantic classes of Identification are No Anomaly (in the channels listed), One Channel (small), One Channel (large), Two Channels, Three Channels, with the single variate answer choices ordered lexicographically. 

Start Time. The semantic classes of Start Time are No Anomaly, Earliest, Early, Medium, Late, corresponding to the temporal order of the answer choices presented. 

End Time. The semantic classes of End Time are No Anomaly, Early, Medium, Late, Latest, corre-sponding to the temporal order of the answer choices presented. 

Magnitude. The semantic classes of Magnitude are No Anomaly, Smallest, Small, Medium, Large, corresponding to the magnitude order of the answer choices presented. 

Categorization. The semantic classes for Categorization are No Anomaly, Change in Trend, Transient Spike, Level Shift, Change in Seasonality/Variance. 

Correlation. The semantic classes match the answer choices exactly. These include: No, there is no anomaly in either time-series, No, there is an anomaly in both but they are not correlated, No, there is an anomaly only in time-series 1, No, there is an anomaly only in time-series 2, Yes, there is an anomaly in both and they are correlated. 

Indicator. The semantic classes match the answer choices exactly. These include: The anomaly in time-series 1 is a leading indicator of the anomaly in time-series 2, The anomaly in time-series 1 is a lagging indicator of the anomaly in time-series 2, The anomaly in time-series 1 is not correlated to the anomaly in time-series 2, The anomaly in time-series 1 is perfectly correlated to the anomaly in time-series 2, No Anomaly in one or both series. 20 C Additional Details on LLM Settings for Inference 

Inference. To evaluate each of the foundation models, we used one of two methods. First, closed-source models were accessed through their respective APIs: Claude Sonnet 4.5 and Claude Opus 4.6 was evaluated through the Anthropic API Anthropic (2025), GPT-5, GPT-5.4, GPT-4.1, and GPT-4o were evaluated with the OpenAI API OpenAI (2023), and Gemini 3 Pro Preview was evaluated through the Google Gemini API. Open-weight models including Qwen3-VL 8B, 32B, Qwen3 32B were evaluated via the vLLM library Kwon et al. (2023), while models such as ChatTS, OpenTSLM, Toto-1.0-Qwen3, Toto-1.0-QA-Experimental were evaluted with Transformers. All models were evaluated with eight NVIDIA A100 GPUs with tensor parallel, using weights downloaded from Hugging Face Transformers Wolf et al. (2019). In terms of model choice, we preferred newer models due to higher overall accuracy on multimodal benchmarks. For the OpenTSLM series, we choose the soft-prompt (SP) Llama-3.2 1B model due to its high test performance on benchmarks reported in the OpenTSLM paper Langer et al. (2025). For inference, we use temperature 0.05 for all third-party models for factuality and determinism (except GPT-5, GPT-5.4, Gemini 3 Pro, which had default temperature 1.0 and is unconfigurable by the API) with maximum token output of 2000 tokens. For reasoning models, we set their reasoning level to “medium” with 2000 tokens. We tested using “high” with 10000 tokens and found similar results to the ones reported, however, we include only the 2000 token results for fairness of comparison with other models. In cases where reasoning models gave blank answers due to reaching or surpassing the maximum output tokens, we labeled such answers as incorrect. For the Toto-1.0-QA-Experimental and Toto-1.0-Qwen3 models, we use temperature 0. Due to context length and channel quantity limits on ChatTS, we make several preprocessing decisions before inputting the time series to the model. To process time series longer than 1024, we subsample the time series down to 1024. For time series with more than 50 variates, we select the 50 variates with the highest mean from each time series in the case of single time series questions, and the 25 variates with the highest mean from each time series in the case of paired time series questions. For the GPT-5 text and Qwen3 32B models, which have a longer context length, we first find a single optimal interval for which the entire series can fit in the context length, and if not, we instead truncate a fraction of the time series data from the middle of the series at various truncation ratios (25%, 40%, 55%, 70%) until we achieve the desired context. 

Multiple Choice Shuffling. At inference time, we randomly shuffle the answer choices before building the prompt in order to prevent option order bias that may result from any one instantiation of ARFBench. 

Plot Generation. In order to evaluate vision-language models with time series data, we transform each time series into a plot with Matplotlib (for single series) and Plotnine (for paired series) with 100 dots per inch (DPI) and then downsize each plot to have a maximum size of 1500 pixels on each side in order to satisfy API requirements. For Qwen3-VL we allow models to auto-resize the images using the built-in Qwen utility tools. For Tier I and Tier II images, the model receives one image as input, corresponding to the time series. We only include channel names in the plot for Identification questions. In Tier III, we input three images: the first image has both time series stacked vertically with a shared time axis. The channels are monochromatic, i.e. all channels of the first time series are blue and all channels of the second time series are orange. The last two plots are plots of each individual time series, in the order they are referenced in the question. Furthermore, the channel names of the time series are omitted from the time series plot in order to retain image resolution and clarity, unless there are less than 8 channels. The channel names are provided in other components (e.g. prompt or question) of the textual input for all QA pairs.

We trained three types of models: vision-language models, TSFM-LLM models, and TSFM-VLM models.

Synthetic data was generated by generating scaled random Gaussian noise time series of various lengths, from 240 to 50,000 timestamps, with 40% of series distributed within 1000-10,000 timestamps. ## F Error Analysis and Case Studies 

We qualitatively analyze the errors made by the models on the benchmark to further understand model output. To do this, we have the model output a reasoning explanation. We sample 50 such errors across all models and categorize them into three different error categories: incorrect perception, instruction following errors, and limited context usage errors. 

Incorrect Perception (48%). These errors result from incorrect analysis of the time series. This includes hallucinating time series values or time ranges, identifying the wrong anomaly time range, or reasoning about incorrect details regarding the time series itself. 

Limited Context Usage (42%). These errors result when not all provided information is utilized. For example, models may neglect processing all channels, leading to information loss. Without utilizing all channels, the model may not be able to contextualize the anomaly properly. In general, this can include incorrect multivariate understanding, lack of caption usage, or lack of domain knowledge. 

Instruction Following Errors (10%). These errors occur from incorrect instruction following. For example, in many Correlation questions, models answer “The anomaly in time series 1 is not correlated to the anomaly in time series 2” even after deducing that there is no anomaly one of the time series. We highlight several case studies (Figures 8, 9, 10, 11) and present them below.
