# Abstract 

Multiscale spatial structure complicates tempo-ral prediction because small-scale spatial fluctua-tions influence large-scale evolution, yet resolving all scales is often intractable. Standard diffusion models do not address this problem effectively since they apply uniform decay across all Fourier modes. We propose Predictor-Driven Diffusion, a framework that combines renormalization-group-based spatial coarse-graining with a path-integral formulation of temporal dynamics. The forward process applies scale-dependent Laplacian damp-ing together with additive noise, producing a hi-erarchy of coarse-grained fields indexed by diffu-sion scale λ. Training minimizes the Kullback– Leibler divergence between data-induced and predictor-induced path densities, leading to a sim-ple regression loss on temporal derivatives. The resulting predictor captures how eliminated small-scale components statistically influence large-scale evolution. A key insight is that the same predictor provides a path score for reverse-λ sam-pling, unifying simulation, unconditional genera-tion, and super-resolution in a single framework. Our unified approach is validated through experi-ments on two multiscale turbulent systems. 

# 1. Introduction 

Many natural and engineered systems exhibit hierarchical structure, where phenomena at different levels of detail in-fluence each other (Levin, 1992; Bar-Yam, 2004; Newman, 2012; Alexakis & Biferale, 2018). For example, weather patterns are shaped by smaller-scale processes such as cloud formation (Bony et al., 2015). In signal processing, Lapla-cian pyramids exploit this structure by decomposing images into coarse components plus progressively finer residuals (Burt & Adelson, 1983; Moulin, 2009). Such hierarchical representations are valuable for modeling and prediction

because different levels interact (Mallat, 1989; E, 2011; Bengio et al., 2013). One way to quantify this hierarchy is through spatial Fourier decomposition (Hussaini & Zang, 1987; Zhang & Lu, 2004): since wavenumber is inversely proportional to wavelength, modes at small and large wavenumbers capture large-scale and small-scale structure, respectively (e.g., Bracewell, 2000). A system is spatially multiscale when it contains Fourier modes spanning a wide range of wavenumbers. This Fourier-based view provides a useful foundation for data analysis and machine learning on multiscale data (Rahimi & Recht, 2007; Mallat, 2016; Li et al., 2021). Diffusion models have emerged as powerful tools for genera-tion (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021), in part because their coarse-to-fine denoising appears to use multiscale structure (Karras et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022b). In standard dif-fusion, small-scale components tend to be corrupted faster in terms of signal-to-noise ratio, so the reverse (generation) process proceeds from large-scale to small-scale structure (Rissanen et al., 2023; Falck et al., 2025). However, this multiscale hierarchy arises implicitly from noise: the model denoises all Fourier modes simultaneously rather than sepa-rating scales explicitly. This means standard diffusion does not fully exploit spatial multiscale structure (Sheshmani et al., 2025; Masuki & Ashida, 2025). In physics, the renormalization group (RG) is a tradi-tional method to address multiscale structure (Wilson, 1971; Berges et al., 2002). RG builds a fine-to-coarse hierarchy by progressively integrating out small-scale degrees of free-dom while preserving their statistical influence on large-scale components. Recent work has combined RG with diffusion models, incorporating scale-aware damping and noise into the forward process (Cotler & Rezchikov, 2023; Sheshmani et al., 2025; Masuki & Ashida, 2025). The re-verse (generation) process then naturally corresponds to super-resolution—reconstructing small-scale structure from coarse representations. Modeling dynamical systems is a fundamental task in physics and engineering, yet existing RG-based diffusion models have focused on static data such as images (Cotler & Rezchikov, 2023; Sheshmani et al., 2025; Masuki & Ashida, 2025). Extending RG-based diffusion to spatiotemporal dy-namics is not straightforward. Coarse-graining (or smooth-1

A key open problem is to achieve this ex-tension while preserving both causality and explicit scale awareness. We address this problem by proposing Predictor-Driven Diffusion . The key idea is to learn a predictor of forward evolution in physical time and use it to define a probability distribution over spatiotemporal trajectories across multiple spatial scales. The predictor computes future states only from past observations, so the framework preserves causal-ity by construction. This approach enables diffusion-like sampling across spatial scales, unifying simulation, genera-tion, and super-resolution within a single model. Our main contributions are: • A joint treatment of physical time (causal evolution) and spatial scale (RG-based hierarchy) as distinct axes, producing coarse-grained fields at multiple resolutions (Sections 2 and 3). • A training objective based on KL divergence between trajectory distributions, which theoretically yields a predictor capturing the influence of small scales on large-scale evolution (Section 3). • A unified framework where a network trained for simu-lation also supports unconditional generation and super-resolution without retraining (Sections 3 and 4). The framework integrates naturally with existing predictor architectures, requiring minimal modifications. We demon-strate these capabilities on chaotic systems with one- and two-dimensional (1D and 2D) spatial domains. 

# 2. Background 

We review recent work that combines RG coarse-graining with diffusion models (e.g., Cotler & Rezchikov, 2023). This review is presented in terms of two axes: physical time t, which indexes causal evolution, and diffusion scale 

λ (defined below), which indexes a spatial scale hierarchy. Prior RG-based diffusion has focused on static fields, where only the λ axis is present. We restate those results with explicit dependence on t to prepare for the spatiotemporal extension in Section 3. 

2.1. RG Transformations via Diffusion Processes 

RG coarse-graining can be formulated as a stochastic diffu-sion process in λ (Carosso, 2020). This is one formulation of RG; we adopt it for concreteness. For a broader perspective on RG approaches, see Cotler & Rezchikov (2023). For a vector field uλ := uλ(x, t ), the forward process is

In Fourier space, the mode at wavenumber k is attenuated by a factor exp( −α ∥k∥2 λ), so modes with large ∥k∥ (small-scale structure) decay faster than those with small ∥k∥ (large-scale structure); as λ increases, uλ becomes progressively coarse-grained. A cutoff wavenumber kcut ∼ 1/√αλ sepa-rates retained modes from suppressed ones (Carosso, 2020). Since the cutoff wavelength is proportional to √λ, the diffu-sion scale λ indexes a spatial hierarchy (Appendix A.2). The noise term in Eq. (1) is essential for RG coarse-graining. Without noise ( β = 0 ), the Laplacian alone performs de-terministic smoothing, which simply removes small-scale components (Carosso, 2020). With noise, the correspond-ing Fokker–Planck equation shows that the forward process induces a convolution of the field distribution with a Gaus-sian kernel (Appendices A.2 and A.3), corresponding to marginalization over small-scale degrees of freedom (Wet-terich, 1991; Carosso, 2020). This marginalization preserves the statistical effect of eliminated components on large-scale components, distinguishing RG coarse-graining from sim-ple low-pass filtering.

# 3. Proposed Method 

We propose Predictor-Driven Diffusion , a framework that constructs a diffusion-like generator for spatiotemporal fields from a learned temporal predictor. At each diffu-sion scale λ, the predictor emulates stochastic dynamics in physical time t, using only present and past informa-tion to remain causal. The emulated dynamics induces a tractable surrogate path density. Its score can be computed via automatic differentiation, enabling reverse-λ sampling for spatiotemporal fields. The framework thus unifies simu-lation, unconditional generation, and super-resolution using a single neural network (Fig. 1).

3.4. Inference: Simulation and Generation 

Once f θλ is trained, inference proceeds in two modes: simu-lation and generation. 

Simulation. We fix the diffusion scale λ, provide a coarse-grained initial condition, and integrate the governing equa-tion (6) forward in physical time t using the learned drift f θλ .For deterministic evaluation, we set σλ = 0 .

Generation. For generation, we use the reverse equation with respect to λ:

∂λuλ = α∇2

> x

uλ − β2sλ + βη λ, (11) where sλ := ∇uλ ln pλ({uλ}t) is the path score and 

ηλ := ηλ(x, t ) is white Gaussian noise. After training, 

pλ ≈ qλ; reverse-λ sampling then generates spatiotemporal fields approximately distributed as q0 (= qd). Since pλ in Eq. (7) is defined explicitly by the learned predictor, the score can be computed via automatic differentiation without training a separate score network. This avoids backpropa-gating through repeated evaluations of f θλ in a physical-time rollout; we discuss the resulting computational cost in Ap-pendix B.7. For unconditional generation, we sample a noisy path at large λ and integrate the reverse equation toward λ = 0 ,yielding high-resolution spatiotemporal samples. The same procedure enables super-resolution: we start from a simu-lated coarse-grained path {uλ}t at some λ > 0 and refine it toward λ = 0 .

# 4. Experiments 

We evaluate the proposed framework on two representa-tive chaotic systems: the 1D Lorenz-96 model with two timescales (Lorenz, 1996) and the 2D Kolmogorov flow (Obukhov, 1983). Both exhibit multiscale structures where small- and large-scale components are coupled. For each system, we test simulation, unconditional generation, and super-resolution using a single trained network. The goal is to validate the framework rather than to achieve state-of-the-art performance on specific benchmarks. Implementation details and extended results are provided in Appendices B to D. Code is available at https://github.com/Yuk iYasuda2718/predictor-driven-diffusion .

4.1. Experimental Setup Training Data For both systems, spatiotemporal samples are generated from physics-based simulations in a statis-tically steady regime. Each Lorenz-96 sample has two channels (X, Y ) with a temporal length of 64 snapshots and a spatial length of 128 grid points. We use 3,000 and 100 samples for training and testing, respectively. Each Kolmogorov flow sample has one vorticity channel with a temporal length of 40 snapshots and a spatial resolution of 

40 × 40 . We use 6,000 and 100 samples for training and testing, respectively. 

Neural Network We use a U-Net architecture (Saharia et al., 2022a) for the predictor f θλ . The network takes as input the current state and the four preceding states, con-catenated along the channel dimension. The output is a finite-difference approximation of the time derivative at the current time. Training minimizes the objective in Eq. (9) using local time derivatives, without rollout in t. We also use a Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2021) to confirm that our method is not sensitive to the choice of network architecture; results are reported in Appendix D.5. 

Hyperparameters RG coarse-graining is implemented analytically via the Laplacian-based operator Cλ and co-variance Σλ in Eq. (3). The diffusion scale λ ∈ [0 , 1] is discretized into 1,000 steps ( λmin = 0 and λmax = 1 ); the parameters α and β are selected via grid search: (α, β ) = (0 .1, √2) for the Lorenz-96 model and (0 .3, √6) for the Kolmogorov flow. In the main text, we report results at 

λ = 0 (fine) and λ = 0 .2 (coarse). In Fourier space, Cλ

attenuates modes by exp( −α ∥k∥2 λ), so λ = 0 .2 effec-tively removes modes with kcut ∼ 1/√αλ (about 7 for the Lorenz-96 model and about 4 for the Kolmogorov flow). 

Baseline Models As a baseline, we use a denoising dif-fusion probabilistic model (DDPM) trained with denoising score matching (Ho et al., 2020; Vincent, 2011), using the same U-Net architecture as our model. Separate models are trained for simulation and generation. For simulation, the baseline follows a conditional diffusion setup (e.g., Price et al., 2025), using the same temporal window as our model. Since the baseline does not define a spatial coarse-graining hierarchy, it cannot be evaluated at coarse-grained resolu-tions ( λ > 0); thus, we compare simulations only at λ = 0 .For generation, the baseline produces spatiotemporal sam-ples unconditionally. 

Evaluation Metrics We assess short-term predictive ac-curacy using the relative L2 error and long-term statistical consistency using the relative spatiotemporal spectral error (Jiang et al., 2023). # 5. Related Work 

Diffusion and Flow-Based Generative Models Gener-ative models based on stochastic and ordinary differential equations (SDEs and ODEs) have been extensively stud-ied (Song et al., 2021; Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo et al., 2023). These models treat a diffusion scale λ as a time-like parameter—often called diffusion time (or step)—with a neural network modeling the score or drift. In contrast, our approach models the drift in physical time t. This dis-tinction affects the training objective: standard diffusion models minimize KL divergences defined in diffusion time (Ho et al., 2020; Kingma et al., 2021), whereas our model minimizes a KL divergence between data and surrogate path densities defined in physical time. In many generative models, intermediate representations along the diffusion-time axis are not explicitly interpreted, as their semantic meaning is generally unclear. Conse-quently, recent work has focused on reducing the number of integration steps by tuning sampling strategies or noise schedules (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Karras et al., 2022), or by straightening the transformation trajectory (Liu et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2023). In contrast, our framework em-ploys RG-based diffusion processes, which provide a clear interpretation of intermediate representations: each λ cor-responds to dynamics coarse-grained at a specific spatial scale. 

RG-Based Diffusion Models RG-based diffusion models have been studied for static data such as images (Cotler & Rezchikov, 2023; Sheshmani et al., 2025; Masuki & Ashida, 2025). These models employ damping and noise levels that depend on both wavenumber k and diffusion scale λ; the Laplacian-based formulation we use is one instance of this broader class. These approaches improve computational efficiency and accuracy over standard diffusion (Sheshmani et al., 2025; Masuki & Ashida, 2025). Since we do not optimize damping and noise schedules, our model could similarly adopt k- and λ-dependent damping and noise lev-els to improve efficiency or accuracy, which we leave to future work. 7Predictor-Driven Diffusion for Spatiotemporal Generation 

Other Scale-Aware Diffusion Models Several diffusion models introduce multiscale structure without modifying the forward process. Some use hierarchical networks or multiresolution representations (Ryu & Ye, 2022; Fan et al., 2023; Phung et al., 2023), while others factorize the data dis-tribution across scales and estimate conditional scores (Guth et al., 2022). RG-based diffusion differs fundamentally by incorporating scale dependence in the forward process through explicit coarse-graining. Despite this distinction, both paradigms share the principle of coarse-to-fine genera-tion (e.g., Guth et al., 2022), suggesting potential avenues for integration. 

Neural ODEs and SDEs Neural networks have been used to approximate ODEs or SDEs in physical time t, typically at a fixed resolution without an explicit diffusion scale λ

(Legaard et al., 2023; North et al., 2023). For dynamics prediction, some methods use diffusion time as a proxy for physical time (R ¨uhling Cachay et al., 2023; 2024). For gen-eration, others introduce dynamical-system perspectives into continuous-time neural models (Chen et al., 2018; Kidger et al., 2021). Our method explicitly distinguishes physical time t from the diffusion scale λ, learning a λ-conditioned family of predictors tied to spatial coarse-graining, not a single-resolution surrogate. 

Energy-Based Generative Models Energy-based models define a probability density as p(x) ∝ exp( −E(x)) with a learned energy E(x) (Gao et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2024). These models use differentiable log-densities to support both sampling and learning. Our path-probability formula-tion plays a formally analogous role: the exponent in Eq. (7) serves as a path energy, enabling the computation of path-wise scores for reverse-λ sampling. While energy-based approaches primarily target static data distributions, Shen & Cheng (2025) proposed a likelihood-based framework for temporal dynamics, albeit without explicitly addressing multiscale structure. Our method integrates temporal dy-namics with explicit spatial multiscale modeling through RG coarse-graining. 

Super-Resolution Diffusion models have been widely ap-plied to super-resolution (Ho et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022a;b; Mardani et al., 2025). Some approaches interpret or exploit intermediate diffusion states as coarse-grained representations, and perform super-resolution by integrating from these states along the diffusion-scale axis (Shu et al., 2023; Bischoff & Deck, 2024). When combined with predic-tion tasks, a common strategy is to perform low-resolution surrogate prediction, followed by super-resolution (Chen et al., 2023; Brenowitz et al., 2025), but the predictor and super-resolver are typically trained separately. Our frame-work unifies these steps: the predictor defines a path model whose score enables reverse-λ super-resolution. 

# 6. Conclusions 

We proposed Predictor-Driven Diffusion, a framework that combines RG-based spatial coarse-graining in diffusion scale λ with a path-integral formulation of temporal evolu-tion in physical time t. This construction gives intermediate diffusion states a clear interpretation as a spatial hierarchy of coarse-grained dynamics. The key idea is to train a predictor on the KL divergence between path densities across a range of λ to capture multiscale dynamics. This two-axis construc-tion enables forward-t simulation and reverse-λ sampling, unifying simulation, generation, and super-resolution in a single neural network. We validated the framework on the Lorenz-96 model and Kolmogorov flow, two representative multiscale dynamical systems. 

Limitations Our framework targets spatiotemporal fields governed by physical dynamics; its applicability to general video data without explicit governing equations remains un-explored. We treat the initial density rλ as empirically given, which is appropriate for stationary systems but requires ex-tension for transient settings. We validated the framework only on the 1D Lorenz-96 model and 2D Kolmogorov flow; scalability to higher resolutions and three-dimensional data remains untested. Generation and super-resolution are more costly than simulation due to path-score computation at each reverse-λ step. The coarse-graining parameters α and 

β may require system-specific tuning; adopting λ-dependent 

α (damping) and β (noise amplitude) may further improve accuracy.

Data Preprocessing All state variables (i.e., X, Y, ζ ) are standardized to have zero mean and unit standard deviation. This normalization removes the zero-wavenumber constant mode and eliminates the need for scale-independent damping terms (Cotler & Rezchikov, 2023). 

B.2. Neural Network Architecture U-Net. We use a U-Net-based neural network architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; 2022; Saharia et al., 2022a;b). The number of channels is first increased to 32 by convolution, after which the spatial resolution is progressively reduced. Self-attention mechanisms are then applied (Vaswani et al., 2017), and finally the spatial resolution is restored. For the Lorenz-96 model, one-dimensional convolutions are used to reduce the spatial resolution to 1/16 ( = 1 /24) of the original size, while the number of channels is gradually increased to 64, 128, 128, and 192. For the Kolmogorov flow, two-dimensional convolutions are used to reduce the spatial resolution to 1/8 ( = 1 /23) of the original size, with the number of channels increased to 64, 128, and 192. Downsampling is performed using convolutions with stride 2, and upsampling is performed using transposed convolutions. The Swish activation function is used throughout the network (Ramachandran et al., 2017). The diffusion scale λ is provided via Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM) conditioning (Perez et al., 2018), which modulates intermediate features through learned scale and shift parameters. The input to the neural network consists of the state vectors at the current time and the four most recent past time steps. For the Lorenz-96 model, this corresponds to 10 input channels, representing X and Y over five time steps, while for the Kolmogorov flow the input consists of 5 channels, representing ζ over five time steps. All variables are concatenated along the channel dimension before being provided to the network. The network output is a finite-difference approximation of the time derivative in physical time t. In Appendix D.2, we vary the number of input time steps; the network architecture is kept fixed throughout. 

FNO. As an alternative architecture, we also employ a Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2021). The FNO applies spectral convolutions in Fourier space with mode truncation, followed by pointwise nonlinearities. We use 8 Fourier layers for the Lorenz-96 model and 6 layers for the Kolmogorov flow, with a hidden width of 64 in both cases. The number of retained Fourier modes is 56 (out of 64 Nyquist modes) for the Lorenz-96 model and 16 (out of 20 Nyquist modes) for the Kolmogorov flow. The Swish activation and FiLM conditioning for λ are the same as in the U-Net. The input and output formats are also identical. Unlike the U-Net, the FNO uses neither convolutions nor self-attention, providing a test of whether our proposed method works across different network architectures. Results with FNO are presented in Appendix D.5. 

Temporal boundary handling. For the first few temporal indices where the history window is not fully available, we use zero padding for the missing past states. 

B.3. Hyperparameters Validation set. For hyperparameter tuning, we use 100 samples with distinct initial conditions as the validation set for both the Lorenz-96 model and Kolmogorov flow, separate from both the training and test sets. 22 Predictor-Driven Diffusion for Spatiotemporal Generation 

Coarse-graining. For the Lorenz-96 model, we set α = 0 .1 and β = √2, while for the Kolmogorov flow we set α = 0 .3

and β = √6. The Lorenz-96 model has 128 spatial grid points, which is approximately three times that of the Kolmogorov flow (i.e., 40 grid points per spatial dimension), and therefore a smaller value of α is used for the Lorenz-96 model. We do not set α exactly to 1/9 (= 1 /32), because the Kolmogorov flow is a two-dimensional system and a direct comparison is not straightforward. These parameter values are determined by grid search, and we confirm that the results are not overly sensitive to the exact values. The diffusion scale is taken as λ ∈ [0 , 1] (λmin = 0 and λmax = 1 ), and a discretization with 1,000 steps is used (i.e., ∆λ = 10 −3). 

Optimization. We train all models using Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) with learning rate 2 × 10 −4 and batch size 50 for the Lorenz-96 model and 40 for the Kolmogorov flow. We train for 3 × 10 4 optimization iterations (no epoch-based schedule and no early stopping). We use the default Adam hyperparameters, with no weight decay and no gradient clipping. We use automatic mixed precision (AMP) with FP16. The DDPM baselines in Appendix B.4 use the same optimization settings. 

B.4. Baseline Models 

As a score-based baseline, we train a denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM) using the denoising score matching objective (Ho et al., 2020; Vincent, 2011). We use the same neural network architecture as described in Appendix B.2 and the same training hyperparameters as in Appendix B.3. The forward diffusion process is variance-preserving (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021) with α = 0 and γ = 3 , i.e., ∂λuλ(x, t ) = −γu λ(x, t ) + √2γ η λ(x, t ), and we discretize λ ∈ [0 , 1] 

into 1,000 steps. As in our proposed method, we do not use a λ-dependent noise schedule. Input time series are concatenated along the channel dimension, and we use the same zero padding at temporal boundaries as in Appendix B.2. 

Baseline for Simulation (Conditional Prediction) For simulation, we follow the conditional diffusion setup used in GenCast (Price et al., 2025). The denoiser estimates the score of the standardized temporal difference (residual) conditioned on the five most recent clean states (the current state and the four preceding time steps). Since this baseline uses α = 0 , it does not support simulations at λ > 0; we therefore compare simulations only at λ = 0 . At inference time, we generate the residual one step at a time and roll out in physical time by sliding the conditioning window. 

Baseline for Unconditional Generation For unconditional generation, we model the joint distribution over full spa-tiotemporal fields and draw samples via DDPM-style reverse-λ integration with 1,000 steps (Ho et al., 2022), using the same reverse-λ discretization as the proposed method (Appendix B.6). To evaluate the score at each temporal grid t(n), the denoiser takes a local temporal window (uλ(t(n)), u λ(t(n−1) ), . . . , u λ(t(n−4) )) from the noisy sample, with zero padding where n < 4. Unlike the simulation baseline, no clean conditioning context is used—all five inputs are noisy. During reverse-λ integration, the score is evaluated at every t(n) and the full spatiotemporal field is updated simultaneously.

B.7. Computational Cost 

Each training iteration samples λ and computes a loss on time derivatives at that λ (Algorithm 1); the per-iteration cost is comparable (up to constants) to DDPM-style training when using the same backbone and similar batching strategies (Ho et al., 2020). Simulation at a fixed λ is inexpensive: the trained drift f θλ is evaluated once per physical-time step to advance the state, with no backpropagation required (Algorithm 2). However, generation and super-resolution are more costly because reverse-λ sampling requires computing the path score via automatic differentiation (Algorithm 3). We discuss this cost in more detail below. In our implementation, a spatiotemporal sample {uλ}t is regarded as a single tensor, analogous to a multi-channel image, and the score is obtained as the input gradient of the scalar log-density with respect to this tensor. This is a vector–Jacobian 25 Predictor-Driven Diffusion for Spatiotemporal Generation 

product computed by reverse-mode automatic differentiation (Baydin et al., 2018). Importantly, we do not backpropagate through a physical-time integrator that repeatedly applies f θλ to produce a rollout. Reverse-mode differentiation of a scalar objective typically incurs a small constant-factor overhead over the corresponding forward evaluation, while its storage requirement can grow with the number of intermediate activations (Baydin et al., 2018). In our experiments, we did not observe memory bottlenecks, but memory can become the dominant constraint as the temporal length and model size increase. If needed, standard checkpointing strategies can trade additional computation for reduced activation memory (Chen et al., 2016). Overall, sampling cost scales approximately linearly with the number of reverse-λ steps and the temporal length Nt (since the tensor size grows with Nt). We have not optimized the sampler for wall-clock performance, so there is room for improvement. For instance, reducing the number of reverse-λ steps via sampler or noise scheduling is a direct way to lower cost (Karras et al., 2022; Sheshmani et al., 2025; Masuki & Ashida, 2025); we leave such optimization for future work. 26 Predictor-Driven Diffusion for Spatiotemporal Generation

D.2. Dependence on the Number of Input Time Steps 

It is common in time-series modeling to use multiple past time steps as inputs (e.g., Tashiro et al., 2021; Yuan & Qiao, 2024; Price et al., 2025). However, our choice of five input steps may appear large compared with those in surrogate models for more complex atmospheric flows (Price et al., 2025). Here we discuss this choice based on the Mori–Zwanzig formalism (Zwanzig, 1961; Mori, 1965; Kubo et al., 1991). Figure 11 shows results using only the current time step as input. At λ = 0 , this model yields accurate predictions since the Lorenz-96 model is Markov at fine resolution (Appendix B.1). At λ = 0 .2, however, a single input step fails to reproduce large-scale variability, producing monotonous patterns instead. In contrast, with five input steps, such variability is reproduced (Fig. 2). This result suggests that reproducing large-scale temporal evolution requires a sufficiently long history when small-scale components are eliminated. Indeed, the prediction error decreases as the number of input steps increases (Fig. 12): increasing from 5 to 15 steps reduces the spectral error from approximately 0.6 to 0.3. These results are consistent with the Mori–Zwanzig projection operator formalism (Zwanzig, 1961; Mori, 1965; Kubo et al., 1991). When small-scale components are eliminated by coarse-graining, the feedback they provide to large-scale dynamics must be represented using only the past history of large-scale variables, thereby breaking the Markov property. In time-series modeling, multiple past states are often provided as inputs (e.g., Tashiro et al., 2021; Yuan & Qiao, 2024; Price et al., 2025), likely because including history improves reproduction of large-scale variability. 31 Predictor-Driven Diffusion for Spatiotemporal Generation 

In the main text (Section 4), we use five input time steps to adequately reproduce coarse-grained dynamics. Using fewer steps simplifies the preparation of initial conditions and reduces computational cost, leading to a trade-off between accuracy and efficiency; we adopt five steps as a compromise. D.4. Effect of Noise 

The forward process in Eq. (1) consists of a Laplacian term and a noise term. In Appendix D.3, we studied the role of the Laplacian term; here, we ablate noise injection to examine its effect on simulation, generation, and super-resolution. Specifically, we train a model using deterministically coarse-grained data: 

uλ(x, t ) = Cλu0(x, t ), (68) which is constructed by setting ϵ ≡ 0 in Eq. (3), i.e., uλ = Cλu0 + √Σλ ϵ with ϵ = 0 . We keep β > 0 as in the main experiments and use the same Σλ (and hence, σλ) for a controlled comparison. We train this noise-free model with the objective in Eq. (9). From a theoretical perspective, diffusion induced by noise leads to a convolution of probability distributions, and the influence of small-scale components is reflected in the statistical properties of large-scale components through the effective theory (Carosso, 2020; Cotler & Rezchikov, 2023). In other words, noise statistically represents the eliminated small-scale degrees of freedom. Theoretical details are provided in Appendix A.3. Here, we experimentally investigate the effect of noise. Even without noise, simulations are numerically stable when λ ≈ 0; however, numerical instability increases and prediction fails as λ becomes larger (Fig. 15). It is known that injecting noise during training improves the numerical stability of surrogate models (Pfaff et al., 2021; Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020), and our results are consistent with this observation. 34 Predictor-Driven Diffusion for Spatiotemporal Generation 

When no noise is injected during training, generation fails (not shown in detail). Specifically, the generated spatiotemporal samples look like noise, and the characteristic spatiotemporal patterns are not reproduced. Theoretically, noise induces a convolution of probability distributions, which corresponds to statistically integrating out the eliminated small-scale degrees of freedom (Carosso, 2020; Cotler & Rezchikov, 2023). Data generation via the reverse process can therefore be interpreted as reconstructing small-scale components from a statistically integrated representation (Cotler & Rezchikov, 2023). When noise is not injected during training, such statistical integration does not occur, and the model fails to learn how to reconstruct small-scale components. We further examine whether super-resolution is possible in the absence of noise. Choosing a relatively small diffusion scale for which coarse-grained (low-resolution) simulation is successful, λ = 0 .025 , we apply the super-resolution procedure. As expected, super-resolution also fails in this case (Fig. 16). This result is consistent with the failure of unconditional spatiotemporal data generation and suggests that small-scale components cannot be reconstructed without statistical integration induced by noise.   
