# ODE-free Neural Flow Matching for One-Step Generative Modeling 

## Xiao Shou Baylor University 

Abstract 

Diffusion and flow matching models generate samples by learning time-dependent vector fields whose integration transports noise to data, requiring tens to hundreds of network evaluations at inference. We instead learn the transport map directly. We propose Optimal Transport Neural Flow Matching (OT-NFM), an ODE-free generative framework that parameterizes the flow map with neural flows, enabling true one-step generation with a single forward pass. We show that naive flow-map training suffers from mean collapse , where inconsistent noise–data pairings drive all outputs toward the data mean. We prove that consistent coupling is necessary for non-degenerate learning and address this using optimal transport pairings with scalable minibatch and online coupling strategies. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and image generation tasks (MNIST and CIFAR-10) demonstrate competitive sample quality while reducing inference to a single network evaluation. Keywords: Neural Flow · Flow Matching · Optimal Transport 

# 1 Introduction 

Generative modeling has undergone a remarkable transformation in recent years. Diffusion models [20, 11, 23] demonstrated that high-fidelity synthesis can be achieved by learning to reverse a gradual noising process. Flow matching [14, 24] reformulated this paradigm in a more direct deterministic framework by learning a time-dependent velocity field whose integration transports noise to data. Despite their success, most diffusion and flow-matching methods learn time-dependent local vector fields, scores or velocities, whose numerical integration defines the global transport map. Consequently, sample generation requires iterative ODE/SDE solvers, typically involving tens of neural network evaluations. One-step generation has consequently become a central challenge. Consistency models [22, 21] impose self-consistency constraints along learned trajectories to collapse sampling to a single step, but require careful discretization curricula and can be unstable to train. MeanFlow [9] reformulates the problem through average velocity fields and achieves impressive one-step results from scratch, yet still fundamentally trains a velocity-based model requiring ODE integration at inference. A natural alternative is to sidestep velocity fields entirely and learn the flow map , the direct mapping from initial noise to data at time t, so that generation reduces to a single forward pass with no ODE solver. In this work, we propose Optimal Transport Neural Flow Matching (OT-NFM), a framework that instantiates this idea via neural flows [2], without distillation, consistency constraints, or ODE solvers at inference. We show that naive training of the neural flow map fails: without a structured coupling between noise and data, the squared-loss objective drives the model toward the global data mean — a degenerate solution we term mean collapse . This failure is unique to neural flows, which condition on the fixed initial noise x0 and have no mechanism to self-correct, unlike flow matching which conditions on the evolving state 

xt at each integration step. Optimal transport resolves this by providing each x(i)0 a geometrically consistent target throughout training, making OT necessary rather than merely beneficial — a strictly stronger role than it plays in flow matching. Our contributions are as follows: 

• We propose OT-NFM, an ODE-free generative framework that learns the flow map directly via neural flows, enabling true one-step image generation with a single forward pass and no numerical integration at inference. We further analyze a family of interpolation trajectories and show that linear interpolation is the natural and most efficient choice for neural flow matching. 1

# 2 Related Work 

Diffusion and flow-based generative models. Diffusion probabilistic models [11, 20] learn to reverse a fixed noisy diffusion process, achieving state-of-the-art sample quality at the cost of hundreds of iterative denoising steps. Score-based generative models [23] unify diffusion with stochastic differential equations, en-abling continuous-time formulations and flexible noise schedules. Flow matching [14, 15, 1] replaces stochastic dynamics with a deterministic velocity field governed by an ODE, enabling simulation-free training by re-gressing conditional vector fields along interpolation paths between source and target samples. Optimal transport flow matching [24, 13] incorporates OT couplings to straighten trajectories and reduce the num-ber of inference steps required. LOOM [6] maintains an online OT coupling refined iteratively during flow matching training, approximating the global transport plan without precomputation. Our work departs from all of the above by learning the flow map directly rather than a velocity field, eliminating the need for numerical ODE integration entirely via neural flow [2]. More recently, Curly Flow Matching [18] extends flow matching to non-gradient, periodic dynamics by constructing a Schr¨ odinger bridge with a non-zero drift reference process, enabling trajectory inference for systems such as single-cell RNA cycles that fundamentally cannot be captured by gradient-field methods. 

Few-step and one-step generation. Reducing inference cost from hundreds to few or one function evaluations has been an active research direction. Consistency models [22, 21] enforce self-consistency along ODE trajectories, enabling one- or two-step sampling; however, training can be unstable and requires a carefully designed discretization curriculum. Flow Map Matching [3] provides a principled mathematical foundation for this family by directly parameterizing the two-time flow map of an underlying dynamical model, unifying consistency models, consistency trajectory models, and progressive distillation under a single framework. Shortcut models [8] introduce a self-consistency loss over flow intervals, enabling one-step generation without distillation. Inductive Moment Matching [25] models self-consistency of stochastic interpolants at different time steps, achieving competitive one-step results. Rectified flow [15] proposes iterative reflow to straighten trajectories and accelerate sampling. MeanFlow [9] introduces average velocity fields and derives a principled identity between average and instantaneous velocities, enabling one-step generation from scratch without pre-training or distillation. In contrast to all of these approaches, which either parameterize velocity fields or rely on consistency constraints and ODE solvers at inference, our method directly parameterizes the flow map via neural flows [2] and generates samples in a single forward pass without distillation, iterative refinement, or numerical integration. Our work further distinguishes itself by grounding the flow map in an optimal transport coupling, which we show is a necessary condition for non-degenerate training — a role OT does not play in any of the velocity-based methods above.

## 3.2 Neural Flows 

Neural flows [2] are a class of continuous-time generative models that also describe the evolution of a state 

xt ∈ Rd over time, but differ fundamentally in how this evolution is parameterized. Whereas flow matching [14] (and Neural ODEs [4]) define dynamics implicitly via a velocity field, neural flows parameterize the 

flow map —that is, the solution of the dynamics—directly. Concretely, instead of specifying a differential equation for xt, neural flows model the state evolution explicitly as 

xt = Fθ (t, x0), (3) where Fθ : [0 , 1] × Rd → Rd is a neural network that directly parameterizes the solution map from initial conditions and time to states. To correspond to a valid continuous-time flow and probability path, the mapping Fθ is typically constructed to satisfy the following properties: 

• Identity at the initial time. The mapping recovers the identity at time zero, Fθ (0 , x0) = x0,ensuring that trajectories originate from their specified initial conditions. 

• Invertibility with respect to the state. For any fixed t, the map Fθ (t, ·) is invertible, which guarantees uniqueness of trajectories and prevents intersections between solution curves corresponding to different initial states. Under mild regularity conditions, these properties ensure the existence of an implicit time-dependent vector field whose integral curves are given by Fθ , even though the vector field itself is never explicitly parameterized. 

# 4 Method 

We propose Neural Flow Matching (NFM), a framework that directly parameterizes the flow map Fθ : [0 , 1] ×

Rd → Rd satisfying Fθ (0 , x0) = x0, mapping a noise sample x0 ∼ p0 to a target sample Fθ (1 , x0) ≈ x1 ∼ p1.In contrast to flow matching, which learns a velocity field vθ (xt, t ) and requires numerical ODE integration at inference, NFM learns the transport map itself. This yields three key advantages: (1) generation reduces to a single forward pass Fθ (1 , x0) with no ODE solver; (2) training is supervised directly on endpoint pairs (x0, x1) rather than instantaneous velocities; and (3) any interpolation scheme — linear or nonlinear — can be used to define intermediate supervision without affecting the inference-time parameterization and efficiency. 34.1 Naive Neural Flow Matching 

A natural but naive approach to training neural flows is to regress the flow map Fθ (t, x0) toward intermediate interpolation points between independently sampled source and target points, i.e. π := p0 × p1. Concretely, one samples x0 ∼ p0, x1 ∼ p1 independently, draws t ∼ U (0 , 1), and defines the target state via linear interpolation xt := (1 − t)x0 + tx1 [14, 15, 24]. Theorem 1 reveals a fundamental failure mode unique to neural flows: because Fθ conditions only on the fixed initial point x0, the squared-loss objective drives all trajectories toward the global data mean as 

t → 1, rather than transporting mass toward the full target distribution. This phenomenon arises for any interpolation trajectory (see eq. (12) whenever the coupling is independent. We term this mean collapse , and note that it has no analogue in velocity-based flow matching: there, the model conditions on the evolving state xt at each ODE step and can self-correct inconsistent pairings during integration. Mean collapse therefore motivates replacing the independent coupling with a structured pairing that assigns each x0 aconsistent target throughout training, which we develop in the following section.

Coupling strategies. Theorem 2 establishes that any non-independent coupling suffices for non-degenerate generation, but leaves open the question of which coupling is practical at scale. Exact global OT on N samples requires solving an N × N linear program ( O(N 3)), which becomes intractable for large or high dimensional datasets such as images. We consider three practical alternatives. Minibatch OT [24] precomputes an ap-proximate global assignment by solving sequential non-overlapping B × B subproblems over the dataset; since each subproblem yields an exact permutation matrix, the assignment ˆ σ(i) = j∗ is read directly from each chunk’s solution and fixed for all training steps. Another strategy is LOOM where we [6] maintain a persistent coupling τ initialized to the identity and refines it online via minibatch OT at each training step, gradually converging to the global plan without any precomputation. The third rather crude strategy is Per-batch OT , which solves a fresh B × B problem at each step, providing local optimality within each minibatch but no global consistency. 44.3 Neural Flow Models

## 4.4 Interpolation Trajectories 

A key design choice in NFM is the interpolation scheme used to define the target trajectory xt between source x0 and target x1. Unlike flow matching, which learns a velocity field vθ (xt, t ) that adapts to the current state at each ODE step, neural flows learn a map Fθ (t, x0) conditioned only on the initial state x0.Crucially, this means NFM is not restricted to straight-line trajectories: since Fθ directly models a curve in 

Rd parameterized by t, it can represent any smooth path between x0 and x1 without the trajectory curvature penalty that afflicts ODE-based methods at inference. Given a (OT)-coupled pair ( x0, x1) ∼ π, we consider a general family of interpolation schemes:

# 5 Experiments 

## 5.1 Synthetic Experiments 

We evaluate OT-NFM on four 2-D transport benchmarks designed to probe how coupling quality affects trajectory consistency, multimodal coverage, and the stability of the learned flow map. We compare four coupling strategies: per-batch OT , minibatch OT , LOOM [6], and global OT .

Benchmarks. Gauss → Checkerboard. The source is N (0, I) and the target is a checkerboard of 8 alternating tiles on [ −4, 4] 2. The disconnected target support makes pairing errors immediately visible: mass transported to the wrong tile cannot be corrected at inference. Gauss → Spiral. The target is a two-dimensional Archimedean spiral embedded in a noisy band. This benchmark tests whether the flow map can learn coherent long-range, curved routing from a unimodal source. Gauss → Crescent. The target is a crescent-shaped region requiring the model to learn a strongly asymmetric, non-convex displacement field from a symmetric source. 8-GMM → 2-Moons. The source is an 8-component Gaussian mixture arranged on a circle and the target is the two-moons distribution. This task stresses long-range, multi-to-multi routing: each of the 8 source modes must be stably assigned to one of the two crescents throughout training. 

Results. Figure 1 shows the learned flow trajectories for all four tasks and all four coupling strategies. The results reveal a consistent hierarchy in trajectory quality that is determined almost entirely by the global consistency of the source–target pairing. 

Global OT produces the sharpest results across all tasks. On Gauss → Checkerboard, radial, non-crossing trajectories route each source region to its geometrically nearest tile, yielding crisp tile boundaries. On Gauss → Spiral and Gauss → Crescent, trajectories fan out coherently from the source, smoothly filling the curved and asymmetric target supports. On 8-GMM → 2-Moons, each source mode is committed to a single crescent with no inter-mode crossings. 

Minibatch OT achieves near-identical coverage and trajectory structure on all four tasks. Because each chunk’s local assignment is an exact permutation matrix and is fixed for all training steps, the flow map receives gradient signals that are globally consistent up to the granularity of the chunk size. ## 5.2 Image Generation 

5.2.1 MNIST 

We train our neural flow model on MNIST (28 ×28, grayscale) with precomputed minibatch OT. Since FID is unreliable for 28 ×28 grayscale images, we assess quality through visual inspection and a coupling ablation. 

Coupling ablation. Figure 2 compares samples across methods and coupling strategies. The no-coupling baseline (NF, no OT) collapses entirely to a blurred, nearly identical blob for every noise input which is a direct visual confirmation of the mean collapse described in section 4.1. Replacing the independent coupling with precomputed minibatch OT immediately restores sharp, diverse digit generation, demonstrating that consistent source–target pairing is both necessary and sufficient to avoid collapse. Among the baselines, OT-CFM produces the sharpest strokes overall, benefiting from 100 NFE of iterative correction. Our minibatch OT model generates comparably legible digits in a single forward pass with no ODE solver. MeanFlow digits are recognizable but exhibit thicker, less precise strokes; we attribute this to the fact that the original MeanFlow implementation for image generation relies on Diffusion Transformer (DiT) backbones [17], whereas our comparison uses the same lightweight UNet architecture across all methods for a controlled evaluation. Importantly, all velocity-based baselines (OT-CFM, MeanFlow) remain robust to coupling quality, since iterative inference self-corrects pairing errors at each step — a robustness that neural flows cannot rely on.

## 5.3 Ablation Experiment: Trajectory Inter-polation 

We ablate four interpolation schemes across two benchmarks with global OT coupling fixed, isolating the effect of the trajec-tory on learned flow map quality. The linear trajectory (already shown in Figure 1) serves as our reference in both cases. Figure 5 shows results on 8-GMM → 2-moons (top row) and Gaussian → Checkerboard (bottom row). Cosine trajectories produce wildly curved paths that loop outward before converging, requiring gθ to model large time-varying velocity changes that add unnecessary learning burden. On the harder checkerboard benchmark, cosine trajectories collapse entirely, failing to route mass into the disconnected squares. Polynomial (α=2) trajectories are cleaner but still exhibit a slow start followed by rapid convergence near t=1, introducing non-constant velocity that complicates learning and scatters mass across square boundaries. Stochastic (σ=0 .5) interpolants inject time-dependent noise that regularizes against mean collapse in the absence of OT, but introduce endpoint variance that degrades one-step generation quality since Fθ (1 , x0) no longer targets x1 exactly. # 6 Conclusion 

We introduced Optimal Transport Neural Flow Matching (OT-NFM), an ODE-free framework for one-step generative modeling that learns the flow map directly using neural flows. By avoiding velocity-field parameterization and numerical integration, OT-NFM reduces generation to a single forward pass. We identified mean collapse , a failure mode unique to neural flow models arising from inconsistent noise–data pairings, and showed theoretically that optimal transport coupling is a necessary condition for non-degenerate flow map learning. To make this practical for large-scale datasets, we proposed scalable coupling strategies based on precomputed minibatch OT and online LOOM refinement, both operating at O(B3) cost per batch. Experiments on synthetic transport benchmarks and image generation tasks (MNIST and CIFAR-10) validate the theory: without OT, neural flows collapse to the data mean, whereas consistent OT couplings produce sharp and diverse samples. OT-NFM achieves competitive generation quality while requiring only a single network evaluation at inference. These results suggest that directly learning transport maps, combined with structured coupling via optimal transport, provides a promising direction toward efficient real-time generative modeling.
