## Abstract 

High-dimensional data are often modeled as lying near a low-dimensional man-ifold. We study how to construct diffusion processes on this data manifold in the implicit setting. That is, using only point cloud samples and without access to charts, projections, or other geometric primitives. Our main contribution is a data-driven SDE that captures intrinsic diffusion on the underlying manifold while being defined in ambient space. The construction relies on estimating the diffusion’s infinitesimal generator and its carré-du-champ (CDC) from a proxim-ity graph built from the data. The generator and CDC together encode the local stochastic and geometric structure of the intended diffusion. We show that, as the number of samples grows, the induced process converges in law on the space of probability paths to its smooth manifold counterpart. We call this construction Implicit Manifold-valued Diffusions (IMDs), and furthermore present a numerical simulation procedure using Euler–Maruyama integration. This gives a rigorous basis for practical implementations of diffusion dynamics on data manifolds, and opens new directions for manifold-aware sampling, exploration, and generative modeling. 

## 1 Introduction 

Stochastic differential equations (SDEs) [ 53 ] model continuous-time dynamics under deterministic drift and random perturbations. In machine learning, they underpin a broad range of methods, including Langevin sampling [ 9, 51 ], Bayesian inference [ 68 , 49 ], stochastic optimization [ 70 , 12 ], and score-based generative modeling [ 60 ]. While most existing methods are formulated in Euclidean space Rn, many datasets are better modeled as concentrating near lower-dimensional manifolds of intrinsic dimension d ≪ n [ 28 ]. Under this manifold hypothesis , appropriate stochastic dynamics are naturally described by a diffusion process (Zt)t≥0 over a Riemannian manifold M through the SDE

In this work, we take an operator-theoretic approach to diffusion on implicit manifolds. Starting from a proximity graph built from the point cloud XN , we consider the associated random walk graph Laplacian LN ; the discrete generator of a local Markov process on the graph. We then show that, in the large-sample limit, LN

Moreover, we prove that the constructed process Yt convergence on the space of probability paths C([0 , T ], F (M)) to F (Zt). Figure 1 depicts a simple visual example of our construction on the torus. The above results provide the first fully data-driven path space-consistent construction of diffusion processes on implicit manifolds, which we dub Implicit Manifold-valued Diffusions (IMDs). We prove the graph-to-manifold convergence in Theorem 2, and the consistency of Euler-Maruyama discretization of IMDs in Theorem 3. Numerical experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets are presented in Section 6, showing that IMDs make for a viable lightweight method to both explore and generate new samples from data manifolds. We summarize our contributions below. • Data-driven formulation of manifold diffusion. We show that stochastic processes on arbitrary data manifolds can be constructed by estimating their infinitesimal generators, without access to charts, projections, or explicit geometric information. • Path-space convergence guarantees. We prove that our data-driven generator converges to the Laplace–Beltrami operator and that the induced stochastic process converges in law on path space C[0 , T ] to its smooth manifold counterpart in the large-sample limit. Such contributions find many applications in science, such as computational biology [ 71 , 36 ], robotics [57], material science [72], and neuroscience [54]. • Recovery of intrinsic stochastic dynamics. We show empirically that IMDs produce smooth on-manifold trajectories between data points and explore directions inaccessible to score-only retraction baselines. 

## 2 Related Works 

Manifold Learning Existing methods in the implicit manifold setting typically attempt to first recover the manifold structure, before approximating the dynamics. One approach consists in learning a latent representation [ 2 , 52 ] before simulating Brownian motion on the latent coordinates. The use of Diffusion Maps (DMs) [ 23 ] provides a principled graph-to-manifold limit, which enables latent estimation [ 32 ], or estimation of diffusion generators [ 47 ] for Langevin sampling. Such use of DMs allows for correct sampling of marginals, but not of the entire path. 

Diffusion Geometry A more recent line of work in diffusion geometry [ 40 , 41 ] involves recovering the implicit manifold’s "Riemannian" geometry [ 3 ] in the ambient Euclidean space, which inspired manifold-aware generative modeling methods using the carré-du-champ in flow matching [ 4 , 5 ]. This method is closer in spirit to IMDs, as geometry emerges from the computed operators. 

Score-Based Diffusion Models Another avenue involves the connections between score-based generative models and implicit manifolds [ 55 , 24 , 17 , 15 ]. [ 42 ] proposes score-based retractions as approximate projections onto F (M) to recover manifold operations in the implicit manifold setting. Geometric limits of graph Laplacians This is the theoretical backbone of IMDs, and consists of the main proof techniques. Such limits were pioneered in a series of work by [ 6, 7, 8 ], later refined to pointwise convergence of the random walk graph Laplacian to the Laplace-Beltrami operator by [35]. More recently, spectral convergence was established by considering continuum limits of point clouds [ 63 , 64 , 30 , 31 ], or metric geometry concepts [ 16 ], both of which we will use in the proof of Theorem 2.

66 Experiments 

We evaluate IMDs on both synthetic and real-world data to highlight three key properties: 1. Geometric Fidelity. The IMD, despite being agnostic to the geometry of M, does not deviate from its surface. Moreover, we show that the error does not accumulate. 2. Statistical Fidelity. The law of the generated process on C[0 , T ] matches its theoretical counterpart, providing experimental evidence for Theorem 2. 3. Better Generalization. The tangential dynamics that IMDs provide are strictly orthogo-nal to the "normal" directions of traditional score-based generative models (SGMs). We demonstrate on the MNIST dataset that IMDs allow for a smooth interpolation between two datapoints, which is inaccessible to SGMs. We refer the reader to Appendix F for details on the experimental setups.

We see that IMDs produce coherent transitions between distinct regions of the data distribution, whereas score-based retraction alone fails to do so reliably (Fig. 4c). 87 Limitations and Outlook 

Higher dimensions The computation of the graph Laplacian relies on a kernel kϵ, which inherently suffers from the curse of dimensionality of kernel methods [ 65 ]. We however note that recent work proposes a neural surrogate to compute the CDC Γ, offering an interesting computationally tractable option for larger datasets [5]. 

Non-asymptotic rates Deriving finite sample bounds on the convergence of Theorems 2 and 3 is a clear extension of this work, but requires significant additional theoretical analysis and is thus outside of the scope of this work. We note that some non-asymptotic spectral convergence rates for Laplacians are derived in [69] for the flat torus. 

Generative Modeling We believe the interplay of tangent and normal directions to be a very impor-tant tool in the analysis of generative modeling under the manifold hypothesis. An interesting avenue for future work would involve combining both to consider noisy data, and the evolving geometry along generative dynamics. Furthermore, we believe that extending IMDs to endpoint-conditioned processes (conditioned diffusion, Schrödinger bridges) may offer computationally inexpensive and stable alternatives for exploring data manifolds. 

## 8 Conclusion 

In this work, we introduced Implicit Manifold-valued Diffusions, a mathematically rigorous method-ology to simulate stochastic processes of the diffusion type on data manifolds. We demonstrated that IMDs converge to their smooth theoretical counterpart in the large sample limit, enabling simulations of Langevin-type processes on manifolds only defined through point clouds. Moreover, the proposed tangential dynamics on the data manifold complement current literature on score-based sampling, and provide evidence that tangential manifold dynamics enable coherent cross-basin exploration beyond nearest-neighbour memorization-like behavior.

We compute the random walk graph Laplacian in the same way in both cases, using Eq. (14). The CDC operator is computed by considering its action on coordinate functions. At each timestep ℓ

Remark 5. We also do not use the Diffusion Maps algorithm [ 23 ], as the variable-bandwidth graph Laplacian construction does not fit into the framework presented in Appendix C. We believe this to be an important future direction for rigorous IMD constructions, but leave it for future work. 

F.2 Score Model 

We train a noise-conditional score network sθ (x, σ ) [ 38 ] following the multi-scale noise formulation of [ 58 , 59 ] using denoising score matching. The synthetic data’s network’s training is parametrized with a fully-connected residual architecture on a noise-prediction loss. For MNIST we parametrize the score-model with a U-Net encoder-decoder architecture with skip connections at each spatial resolution [56, 42]. For the noise conditioning, we embed the noise level via a Gaussian random Fourier feature layer [60 ] and inject it as a bias into the first linear layer (MLP for synthetic data) or as a per-channel bias at every convolutional block (U-Net for MNIST). We use a geometric schedule of noise levels with 20 different noise levels ranging ( σmin = 0 .005 , σ max = 1 ) for the synthetic examples and 100 noise levels ranging ( σmin = 0 .01 , σ max = 15 ) for MNIST. 

Training Details For the synthetic data, we trained a 3 residual layer network of 128 hidden dimensions for 200 epochs with a fixed learning rate of 2 × 10 −4 on the Adam optimizer. For MNIST, we trained a three-level U-Net operating on single-channel 28 ×28 images, with skip connections at every resolution and channel widths of 64 , 128 , and 256 . The model is trained for 1,000 epochs using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 2 × 10 −4 and batch size 256 .

Noise Scale Selection The noise scale σ in the DRGD step of Algorithm 1 is chosen to be small enough such that no numerical errors are incurred during score estimations (as in [ 42 ]). In particular, we chose σ = 0 .005 for all synthetic tasks, and σ = 0 .01 for the MNIST tasks. 

F.3 Euler-Maruyama Parameters 

As the Euler-Maruyama scheme is fairly simple, we only discuss choice os step size and an extra parameter we call the speedup. All experiments were performed with a step size h = 10 −3. However, as certain datasets were not normalized (as is the unit sphere S2), we included a speedup term, which effectively stretches the step size. This was done in order to avoid increasing the "physical time" T

too drastically, while keeping a low number of steps, but allowing a larger step to be covered. In particular, we used a speedup of 1.0 for the sphere, 100 .0 on the swiss roll and 10 .0 on MNIST. The particular number of steps was chosen per experiment, depending on what aspect of IMDs was to be highlighted.

G.2 Swiss Roll 

To test IMDs on implicit manifolds, we considered the Swiss roll dataset. Because neighbours in Euclidean distance may not correspond to those in geodesic distance, unconstrained or projection-based dynamics may induce transitions across nearby folds that are distant along the manifold. IMDs avoid this failure mode by constructing dynamics at the level of the generator, ensuring that motion is intrinsically constrained.
