# ABSTRACT 

Diffusion models often degrade when trained in latent spaces (e.g., VAEs), yet the formal causes remain poorly understood. We quantify latent-space diffusability through the rate of change of the Minimum Mean Squared Error (MMSE) along the diffusion trajectory. Our framework decomposes this MMSE rate into contributions from Fisher Information (FI) and Fisher Information Rate (FIR). We demonstrate that while global isometry ensures FI alignment, FIR is governed by the encoder‚Äôs local geometric properties. Our analysis explicitly decouples latent geometric distortion into three measurable penalties: dimensional compression, tangential distortion, and curvature injection. We derive theoretical conditions for FIR preservation across spaces, ensuring maintained diffusability. Experiments across diverse autoencoding architectures validate our framework and establish these efficient FI and FIR metrics as a robust diagnostic suite for identifying and mitigating latent diffusion failure. 

# 1 Introduction 

Diffusion models have revolutionized generative modeling by transforming noise into structured data through a learned reverse process [ 11 ]. While these models excel in high-dimensional pixel spaces, computational constraints often necessitate their application within the compressed latent spaces of pre-trained autoencoders [ 26 ]. However, a persistent and poorly understood phenomenon remains: diffusion models often degrade or fail entirely when applied directly to the latent spaces of standard autoencoders, including Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) [ 16 , 27 ]. Recent literature refers to this mismatch between diffusion dynamics in pixel space and latent space as the diffusability problem [28]. Current explanations for this failure typically point to the ‚Äúgeometric distortion‚ÄĚ introduced by standard encoders, which warp the data manifold and create regions that diverge from the Gaussian prior assumed by the diffusion process [ 2 , 21 ]. In response, various empirical methods have emerged to mitigate this issue, such as geometry-preserving adaptations (e.g., Geometry Preserving Encoder/Decoder (GPE) [ 20 ]), explicit target latent geometries [ 6 , 22 , 34 ], and modified training objectives [17, 33, 10, 3]. (We provide a comprehensive discussion of these related works in Appendix A.) Despite these empirical advances, the underlying causes of latent diffusion failure remain theoretically obscured. Crucially, the literature lacks a formal, quantitative framework for measuring how ‚Äúdiffusible‚ÄĚ a latent space is before 

committing to the computationally expensive training of a full diffusion model. In this paper, we demystify latent-space diffusability through the rigorous lens of Fisher geometry [ 1 ], complementing recent information-geometric perspectives on diffusion trajectories [ 13 ]. We analyze diffusability via denoising complexity , quantified by the rate of change of the Minimum Mean Squared Error (MMSE) along the continuous diffusion trajectory. We show that this denoising complexity fundamentally decomposes into two terms: the Fisher Information (FI) and its rate of dissipation, which we define as the Fisher Information Rate (FIR). To match pixel-space diffusability, encoders must preserve both FI and FIR. Stabilizing FI requires global near-isometry (Section 3), while stabilizing FIR imposes stricter constraints: the encoder must be locally near-isometric

2.2 Latent Diffusion and the Geometry Gap 

To reduce computational costs, diffusion is often performed in the compressed latent space Z of a pre-trained autoen-coder [ 26 ]. However, standard VAEs regularize Z toward a Gaussian prior using Kullback-Leibler divergence, which frequently causes ‚Äúmanifold crinkling‚ÄĚ [ 2 ]. This phenomenon occurs when distant points on the data manifold X

are mapped to adjacent latent coordinates to satisfy the prior, ensuring that Euclidean distances in Z fail to preserve the intrinsic data geometry. This creates a fundamental ‚Äúgeometry gap‚ÄĚ between the spaces. Recent architectures address these geometric distortions by explicitly regularizing latent representations. For example, Geometry-Preserving Encoders (GPEs) align latent Euclidean geometry with data-space Euclidean geometry [ 20 ], while spherical and manifold autoencoders utilize non-Euclidean latent spaces to better capture complex data topologies [22, 34]. 

2.3 The Diffusability Issue 

A distribution‚Äôs diffusability ‚ÄĒits ease of modeling via diffusion‚ÄĒdepends on the score function‚Äôs smoothness and learnability. Structural irregularities or low-density regions cause poorly calibrated gradients, degrading sample fidelity. 

Spectral Artifacts in Latent Spaces. Compressed latent spaces often exacerbate these irregularities. Spectral analyses show autoencoders introduce high-frequency components or singularities, impeding coarse-to-fine synthesis [ 28 ]. Even with smoother encodings [18, 19], diffusion remains unstable if the encoded manifold in Z lacks structural regularity. 

Limitations of Metric Regularization. Recent techniques constrain the encoder Jacobian to enforce local isometry [ 5, 23 ]. While empirically addressing the geometry gap, they lack a formal information-geometric analysis of diffusability. In this work, we introduced a formal information-geometric framework to demystify the ‚Äúdiffusability‚ÄĚ of latent spaces in generative modeling. By decomposing denoising complexity‚ÄĒquantified by the rate of change of the MMSE‚ÄĒinto Fisher Information (FI) and Fisher Information Rate (FIR), we identified the specific geometric properties required for successful latent diffusion. # A Related Works 

The term ‚Äúdiffusability‚ÄĚ was first introduced by Skorokhodov et al. [ 28 ] to describe the extent to which a latent space facilitates the diffusion process. The authors argued that high-frequency components in latent representations reduce diffusability, though they did not provide a rigorous quantitative measure for this effect. Subsequently, [ 35 ] offered further empirical evidence demonstrating that standard representation encoders often induce latent distributions that are inherently difficult for diffusion models to fit. Several follow-up works have aimed to improve diffusability through specific architectural or training modifications. For instance, [ 17 ] promoted diffusability by enforcing equivariance in the latent representation, while [ 33 ] targeted the corruption robustness of the latents. Taking a different angle, [ 10 ] improved diffusability by training the encoder to produce latents that already resemble mildly noised diffusion states, allowing the diffusion prior to denoise from a smoother, better-aligned distribution. Rather than modifying the latent distribution directly, [ 3] proposed denoising latent features early in the pipeline and using them to condition later pixel-space denoising, effectively simplifying the overall problem. Another distinct line of work focuses on explicitly constraining the latent geometry. In [ 34 ], latent codes are normalized to lie uniformly on a hypersphere. More broadly, alternative geometric priors for autoencoders have gained traction, including the use of hyperbolic latent spaces [6, 22]. While these prior works have successfully improved diffusability through empirical regularization and structural constraints, they largely lack a unified mathematical framework to quantify why certain representation spaces are easier to model than others. In contrast, our work provides a rigorous geometric analysis of diffusability. By explicitly bounding the Fisher Information Rate (FIR) of the encoded distributions, we establish formal guarantees on how linear encoders and geometric constraints fundamentally alter the stability of the diffusion process.

# I Spectra and Connection to Fisher Geometry 

In the main text, we introduce Fisher information (FI) and Fisher information rate (FIR) as tools for analyzing the diffusability of autoencoder representations. In this section, we examine spectral analysis as an alternative perspective for studying diffusability. Corresponding to the experiments in Sections 4.3 and 4.4, which compare FI and FIR between pixel space and latent space, we analyze the power spectra of FFHQ images and of latent representations produced by the GPE, VAE, and NVAE encoders. This analysis provides a complementary view of the signal structure and allows us to contrast the information revealed by spectral behavior with that captured by FI and FIR. We use the same 64 √ó 64 resolution FFHQ images and the same NVAE, GPE, and VAE models trained on FFHQ data as in the experiments in Sections 4.3 and 4.4. For FFHQ images and the NVAE latent representations, which are two-dimensional spatial tensors, we compute the two-dimensional power spectrum by applying the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) over the spatial dimensions. For multi-channel inputs, the resulting spectra are averaged across channels to obtain a single spectrum per sample. For the latent representations produced by the GPE and VAE encoders, the latent variable for each sample is a one-dimensional vector. To analyze their frequency characteristics, we compute the one-dimensional power spectrum using the discrete Fourier transform (FFT) along the latent dimension. Each latent vector is normalized prior to the transform. Both the frequency axis k and the power values P are normalized by the signal length (or spatial resolution for the 2D case). To obtain a stable estimate, the spectrum is averaged over 10 ,000 samples drawn from the dataset. The zero-frequency (DC) component corresponding to the mean signal level is excluded from the plots. Figure 13(a) shows the power spectrum of FFHQ images. Figures (b)‚Äď(d) show the spectra of NVAE latent representa-tions at different spatial resolutions. Although these spectra also decrease with frequency, their shapes differ from the pixel-space spectrum in (a), and the decay behavior varies across resolutions, indicating a mismatch with the spectral structure of the original images. Figures (e) and (f) show the spectra of the GPE and VAE latent representations. Unlike the pixel spectrum in (a), these spectra exhibit strong oscillations and lack a clear monotonic decay, further deviating from the spectral behavior of the original images. Overall, the latent spectra do not always align with the spectrum of the original images. This suggests that spectral statistics alone may not be the most reliable diagnostic for assessing diffusability. In contrast, the FI and FIR analysis in Sections 4.3 and 4.4 provides a more consistent characterization of the diffusability of the corresponding representations. We also note that spectral statistics are not permutation invariant: for example, randomly shuffling the pixels of an image would destroy its spectrum by removing the spatial structure on which the Fourier transform relies. In contrast, FI and FIR depend on the geometry of the probability density through the score norm ‚ą•‚ąá x log p(x)‚ą•2, and are therefore invariant under permutations of coordinates. 29 Understanding Latent Diffusability via Fisher Geometry
