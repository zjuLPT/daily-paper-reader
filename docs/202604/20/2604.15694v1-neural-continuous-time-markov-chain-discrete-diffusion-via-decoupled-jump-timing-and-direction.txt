Abstract 

Discrete diffusion models based on continuous-time Markov chains (CTMCs) have shown strong performance on language and discrete data generation, yet existing approaches typically parameterize the reverse rate matrix as a single object—via concrete scores, clean-data predictions ( x0-parameterization), or denoising distributions—rather than aligning the parameterization with the intrinsic CTMC decomposition into jump timing and jump direction. Since a CTMC is fundamentally a Poisson process fully determined by these two quantities, decomposing along this structure is closer to first principles and naturally leads to our formulation. We propose Neural CTMC , which separately parameterizes the reverse process through an exit rate (when to jump) and a jump distribution (where to jump) using two dedicated network heads. We show that the evidence lower bound (ELBO) differs from a path-space KL divergence between the true and learned reverse processes by a θ-independent constant, so that the training objective is fully governed by the exit rate and jump distribution we parameterize. Moreover, this KL factorizes into a Poisson KL for timing and a categorical KL for direction. We further show that the tractable conditional surrogate preserves the gradients and minimizers of the corresponding marginal reverse-process objective under standard regularity assumptions. Our theoretical framework also covers masked and GIDD-style noise schedules. Empirically, while the uniform forward process has been explored in prior work, our model, to our best of the knowledge, is the first pure-uniform method to outperform mask-based methods on the OpenWebText dataset.To facilitate reproducibility, we release our pretrained weights at 

https://huggingface.co/Jiangxy1117/Neural-CTMC .

# 1 Introduction 

Diffusion models have achieved remarkable success in continuous domains such as images [Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015, Ho et al., 2020, Song et al., 2021b] and audio, establishing score-based generative modeling as a dominant paradigm. Extending this success to discrete data—language [Li et al., 2022, Gulrajani and Hashimoto, 2023], proteins [Alamdari et al., 2023, Avdeyev et al., 2023], and molecular graphs [Vignac et al., 2023]—is an active and challenging research direction, since the continuous score function and Langevin dynamics that underpin continuous diffusion have no direct discrete counterpart. Discrete diffusion models formulate the forward corruption and reverse generation as continuous-time Markov chains (CTMCs) on a finite state space S = {1, . . . , S }. D3PM [Austin et al., 2021] and Multinomial Diffusion [Hoogeboom et al., 2021] initiated this line by introducing discrete transition matrices in discrete time. Campbell et al. [2022] extended the framework to continuous time, deriving a variational bound from CTMC path measures. Subsequent work has explored different parameterizations of the reverse rate matrix 

Rθt (i, j ): SEDD [Lou et al., 2024] learns a concrete score ratio qt(j)/q t(i) analogous to continuous score matching [Hyvärinen, 2005, Meng et al., 2022]; MDLM [Sahoo et al., 2024] simplifies training via clean-data

These methods have achieved increasingly strong results on language modeling benchmarks. Despite this progress, a fundamental structural property of CTMCs has not been directly reflected in the reverse-process parameterization. Any rate matrix admits a unique factorization

This exit-rate/jump-distribution decomposition is precisely the basis of Gillespie’s algorithm [Gillespie, 1977, Anderson, 2007] for exact CTMC simulation: first draw a holding time from the exit rate, then choose the next state from the jump distribution. Despite being classical, this structure has not been reflected in the reverse-process parameterization of discrete diffusion models—existing methods still learn the reverse process through proxy quantities such as score ratios, clean-data predictions, or denoising distributions, which implicitly determine Rθt as a monolithic object. Inspired by the algorithmic decomposition in Gillespie’s algorithm, our starting point is that the training objective itself also decomposes along this timing/direction structure, suggesting a parameterization and training rule that make this separation explicit. Moreover, the two mainstream forward processes in discrete diffusion are masked (absorbing) and uniform. The masked forward process is dominant in current state-of-the-art methods [Sahoo et al., 2024, von Rütte et al., 2025], but once a token is absorbed into the mask state it cannot be revisited, preventing the model from correcting earlier decisions during generation [von Rütte et al., 2025]. The uniform forward process avoids this limitation by allowing transitions among all S states, yet it has received comparatively little attention and generally yields weaker results [e.g., Lou et al., 2024, Austin et al., 2021, von Rütte et al., 2025], among others. We propose Neural CTMC , which addresses both gaps. First, we separately parameterize the exit rate 

λθt and the jump distribution rθt for the reverse process via two network heads. We show that the θ-dependent part of the ELBO is equivalent to a reverse-process KL divergence up to a θ-independent constant, and that this KL naturally decomposes as 

KL Poi (ˆλ∥λθ ) + ˆλ · KL Cat (ˆ r∥rθ ),

a Poisson KL for jump timing and a categorical KL for jump direction. This decomposition is not introduced ad hoc at the architecture level; rather, it is induced by the CTMC path-space objective itself, which suggests learning timing and direction with separate heads. Notably, when specialized to the masked (absorbing) forward process, our objective recovers the MDLM loss exactly, showing that the decomposed formulation strictly generalizes existing approaches. Second, equipped with this decomposed parameterization, Neural CTMC with a pure uniform forward process surpasses several state-of-the-art masked-based methods on the benchmarks we study. # 3 Related Work 

Discrete diffusion models. D3PM [Austin et al., 2021] and Multinomial Diffusion [Hoogeboom et al., 2021] introduced discrete diffusion using transition matrices in discrete time. Campbell et al. [2022] extended this to continuous time via CTMCs, deriving the ELBO from path measure ratios. Subsequent work explored different parameterizations of the reverse rate matrix: SEDD [Lou et al., 2024] uses a concrete score analogous to continuous score matching [Song et al., 2021b, Meng et al., 2022], MDLM [Sahoo et al., 2024] simplifies masked diffusion with x0-prediction, and GIDD [von Rütte et al., 2025] unifies these under a general interpolating framework. Recent concurrent work has also explored discrete flow matching [Campbell et al., 2024, Gat et al., 2024], simplified masked objectives [Shi et al., 2024, Ou et al., 2025], guided generation in discrete spaces [Nisonoff et al., 2024], and improved sampling via informed correctors [Zhao et al., 2025]. None of these methods decompose Rθt into exit rate and jump distribution. 4Rate-matrix factorization. The decomposition of a CTMC rate matrix into exit rate and jump distribution is classical in the theory of Markov chains [Norris, 1997, Jacobsen, 2006], and the Poisson KL KL Poi (λ∥λ′)

arises naturally between Poisson processes of different intensities. The τ -leaping algorithm for simulating chemical kinetics [Gillespie, 1977, Anderson, 2007] exploits a similar decomposition by separately sampling the number of reactions (Poisson) and reaction types (categorical), but this structural insight has not been transferred to generative modeling. Our Corollary 4.4 shows that the path-space KL decomposes into 

KL Poi + KL Cat terms, connecting classical CTMC theory with modern discrete diffusion. 

Variational bounds. The ELBO for continuous diffusion was established via SDE path measures and Girsanov’s theorem [Song et al., 2021a, Kingma et al., 2021]. The discrete analog replaces SDEs with CTMCs and Girsanov’s theorem with the Campbell–Mecke formula [Jacobsen, 2006]. Sun et al. [2023] derived a score-based continuous-time objective for discrete diffusion but did not decompose it into timing and direction components. Our derivation follows this program, obtaining an ELBO whose θ-dependent part is a reverse-process KL that naturally factorizes into a Poisson KL and a categorical KL.

jump destinations from rθt . This naturally exploits the exit-rate/jump-direction decomposition and allows multiple jumps per step. Euler (Algorithm 3) constructs a one-step categorical transition by setting 

pj = λθt (xt) · rθt (j | xt) · τ for j̸ = xt, requiring λθt (xt)τ ≤ 1 to remain a valid distribution. Pseudocode for both samplers is given in Appendix D.

# 6 Conclusion 

We presented Neural CTMC, a discrete diffusion framework that parameterizes the reverse CTMC through separate exit-rate and jump-distribution heads, aligning the model with the intrinsic timing/direction structure of CTMC dynamics. We showed that the θ-dependent part of the ELBO differs from a reverse-process path-space KL only by a constant, that this KL factorizes into a Poisson KL for timing and a categorical KL for direction, and that the tractable conditional surrogate preserves the gradients and minimizers of the corresponding marginal objective under standard regularity assumptions. Empirically, this decomposition makes the pure uniform forward process competitive on the language modeling benchmarks we study. # D Additional Experimental Details 

Datasets. We evaluate on two datasets: (1) TinyStories [Eldan and Li, 2023], a corpus of simple short stories for language modeling; (2) OpenWebText (OWT) [Radford et al., 2019], a large-scale web text corpus. Both are tokenized with the GPT-2 BPE tokenizer (vocabulary size 50257) and truncated or padded to a maximum sequence length of 512. 

Architecture. Neural CTMC uses a 12-layer DiT-style Transformer with hidden dimension 768, 12 attention heads, and a time-conditioning dimension of 128. The vocabulary embedding is rounded up to 50304 (the nearest multiple of 128) for hardware efficiency. The total parameter count is approximately 163M. 

Baselines. For TinyStories, we train MDLM and GIDD from their official source code using the same architecture and training budget as Neural CTMC. For OpenWebText, we use the official released checkpoints of SEDD (682B training tokens), MDLM (262B), and GIDD (262B). GIDD is evaluated with its best interpolation parameter punif = 0 .1. Tokenization and maximum sequence length are matched across methods; for OpenWebText, we regard the resulting comparison as best-effort because the released checkpoints are not trained under a completely identical pipeline. 24 Evaluation. All methods are evaluated using the same protocol: we generate 1024 unconditional samples per configuration and score them with a pretrained Gemma-2 model to obtain generative perplexity (PPL). This measures sample quality under a common external language model, rather than the ELBO-based perplexity reported in some prior work. Because the estimate is computed from a finite sample set, we report the resulting values using ≤ notation in the tables and discussion. For TinyStories, we report PPL over training epochs with 50 sampling steps. For OpenWebText, we sweep the number of sampling steps over 

{16 , 32 , 64 , 128 } at the final checkpoint.
