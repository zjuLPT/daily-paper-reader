Abstract 

Long-term time series forecasting requires models that simultaneously capture rapid oscillations, medium-range periodicities, and slowly evolving macro-trends from a fixed look-back window. Existing lightweight MLP-based models typi-cally operate on a single temporal resolution, limiting their ability to explicitly model patterns at multiple scales. We propose MSMixer , a channel-independent multi-scale MLP architecture that addresses this limitation through three com-plementary innovations: (i) three parallel scale branches at down-sample factors 

{1×, 4×, 16 ×} with independent MLP blocks, (ii) a learnable softmax gate that dynamically weighs branch outputs, and (iii) a DLinear complementary short-cut that provides full-window trend and seasonality context. MSMixer contains only 112 K parameters at H=96 and runs at O(T ) complexity. Evaluated on four ETT benchmarks with standard chronological splits and three random seeds, 

MSMixer achieves the lowest average MSE ( 0.357 ) among lightweight models, outperforming DLinear ( 0.386 , −7.4 % ) and NLinear ( 0.365 , −2.1 % ), win-ning 12 of 16 configurations. Against five Transformer-based baselines from the literature, MSMixer achieves best or second-best MSE in 9 of 16 configura-tions while using 5× fewer parameters than PatchTST. On ETTm1 at H=336 ,

MSMixer (0.367 ) outperforms PatchTST ( 0.369 ); on ETTm2 at H=720 ,

MSMixer (0.358 ) outperforms PatchTST ( 0.362 ). Ablation and sensitivity analyses confirm the complementary contributions of the multi-scale branches and the DLinear shortcut.

# 1 Introduction 

Time series forecasting is a foundational task in engineering and science, with direct applications to electricity load management, climate modelling, financial risk estima-tion, and industrial process control [1, 3]. The key challenge—particularly for long forecast horizons H ≥ 96—is that the target signal is a superposition of components operating at fundamentally different temporal scales. For hourly electricity transformer temperature data (the ETTh benchmarks [1]), the dominant components are: a macro-trend varying over weeks to months, a daily sinusoidal pattern with 24-hour period, a weekly envelope, and high-frequency measurement noise. A model that captures only one of these scales will systematically underfit the others. Two dominant modelling paradigms have emerged in the recent literature. Transformer-based models [1, 3, 5, 6] use attention to model long-range dependencies, but suffer quadratic complexity and large parameter budgets ( >500K). Lightweight MLP-based models [7, 8] achieve efficient O(T ) inference, but existing formulations typically operate on a single temporal resolution—either the full look-back window (DLinear) or a fixed patch scale (PatchTST). TimeMixer [8] introduced multi-scale decomposition with MLP-Mixer blocks and achieved strong results, but at higher parameter count and requiring multiple rounds of down-sampling with learnable mixing across decomposed sub-series. We ask: can we achieve multi-scale representational benefit efficiently, by using simple average-pooled branches at fixed scales combined with a learnable merge? 

This paper proposes MSMixer , a deliberately simple architecture that explores this question. The key contributions are: 

• Multi-scale branch design. Three parallel branches at down-sample factors 

{1×, 4×, 16 ×} each apply a two-layer MLP on their respective resolution, special-ising on fine-grained local detail (1 ×), medium-range cycles (4 ×), and coarse trend (16 ×). 

• Learnable softmax scale gate. A single parameter vector γ ∈ R3 (via soft-max) merges branch outputs, allowing the model to adapt scale emphasis to dataset characteristics without manual tuning. 

• DLinear complementary shortcut. A decomposition-based linear shortcut cov-ers the full T = 336 context, complementing the down-sampled branches which lose global context at 4 × and 16 × resolution. 

• Minimal footprint. 112 K parameters at H=96, O(T ) inference. Among lightweight models, MSMixer wins 12 of 16 ETT benchmark configurations. Against Transformer baselines, it achieves best or second-best MSE in 9 of 16 configurations with 5 × fewer parameters. 

# 2 Related Work 

We position MSMixer within five research threads: Transformer-based forecasters, lightweight MLP and linear models, multi-scale architectures, decomposition methods, and normalisation strategies. 22.1 Transformer-Based Forecasting 

The seminal Transformer [2] inspired a generation of sequence forecasters. Informer [1] reduced attention to O(T log T ) via ProbSparse sampling. Autoformer [3] introduced trend–seasonality auto-correlation decomposition. FEDformer [4] applied frequency-domain random mixing. PatchTST [5] applied a vanilla Transformer channel-independently on 16-step patches, dramatically cutting effective sequence length. iTransformer [6] inverted the attention axis, treating variates as tokens for cross-channel modelling. TimesNet [9] converted 1D time series into 2D tensors via learned period lengths and applied inception blocks, achieving strong multi-task results. These models achieve strong accuracy but carry > 500 K parameters and O(T 2) complexity in their core attention modules. 

2.2 Lightweight MLP and Linear Models 

DLinear [7] demonstrated that two linear layers on trend/residual components can match Transformers with negligible parameters. NLinear [7] applies a last-value nor-malisation before a single linear projection, providing a strong baseline that accounts for non-stationarity. FITS [10] achieved < 10 K parameters via frequency interpola-tion. TSMixer [11] applied alternating temporal and channel MLP-Mixer blocks with strong results. TimesFM [12] proposed a decoder-only foundation model pre-trained on large-scale time series corpora for zero-shot forecasting. TimeMixer [8] is clos-est to our approach: it decomposes inputs at multiple resolutions and mixes across scales. MSMixer differs by using fixed average-pooling for down-sampling (rather than adaptive decomposition), independent MLP blocks per scale (rather than inter-scale mixing), and adding a DLinear shortcut to recover global context from the full window. 

2.3 Multi-Scale and Hierarchical Models 

Multi-scale processing has a long history in computer vision (ResNet [16], FPN) and time series classification (ROCKET [17], HIVE-COTE). For LTSF, N-BEATS [18] used hierarchical trend/seasonality basis expansion. N-HiTS [19] extended it with hierarchical interpolation and multi-rate sampling, achieving strong long-horizon results. SCINet [20] applied interactive convolutions at binary-tree multi-resolution levels. Our approach is simpler than SCINet: we use a flat (non-hierarchical) three-branch structure with average pooling, avoiding the complexity of tree-structured computation. 

2.4 Decomposition-Based Models 

Autoformer, FEDformer, and N-BEATS all decompose signals into trend and residual components. DLinear is the canonical lightweight decomposition model. MSMixer 

takes a different approach: instead of explicit spectral or moving-average decompo-sition, multi-scale mixing implicitly separates components by resolution, with the coarsest scale (16 ×) acting as a natural trend extractor. 32.5 Normalisation Strategies in LTSF 

Distribution shift is a major challenge in long-term forecasting: the statistics of the training look-back window differ from those of the evaluation window. Three mainstream normalisation strategies address this. 

Reversible Instance Normalisation (RevIN) [13] normalises each input window to zero mean and unit variance, and denormalises predictions using the same window’s statistics. It is used by MSMixer and TimeMixer. 

Sample-wise Adaptive Normalisation (SAN) [22] learns an affine normaliser condi-tioned on an adaptive basis of seasonal-trend components, providing richer adaptivity at the cost of additional parameters. 

Dish-TS [21] decomposes each variate into two different statistics (coarse and fine granularity) and calibrates predictions accordingly, handling series with multiple inter-level trend changes. 

MSMixer uses RevIN for its simplicity and proven effectiveness on ETT. Replacing it with SAN or Dish-TS is straightforward and left as future work. 

2.6 Multi-Scale Architectures in Vision 

The success of multi-scale representations in computer vision is well documented. Fea-ture Pyramid Networks (FPN) [23] aggregate features at different spatial resolutions to detect objects at multiple scales. U-Net [24] uses symmetric encoder-decoder paths at multiple resolutions, combining coarse semantic features with fine-grained localisation via skip connections. Swin Transformer [25] hierarchically partitions image patches at 1×, 2 ×, and 4 × sub-sampling, producing naturally multi-scale representations. These architectures validate the design principle that multi-scale feature extraction benefits diverse pattern recognition tasks. MSMixer instantiates the same principle in the 1D time series domain: the parallel scale branches at {1, 4, 16 } correspond to FPN levels, the DLinear shortcut corresponds to the skip connection in U-Net, and the softmax gate corresponds to the adaptive feature re-weighting in Swin. The key adaptation is replacing 2D spatial pooling with 1D temporal average pooling, and replacing convolutional filters with two-layer MLPs.

# 5 Experiments 

We evaluate MSMixer on four ETT benchmarks, comparing against two reproduced lightweight linear baselines and five Transformer-based models from the literature. Ablation and sensitivity analyses examine each design choice. 95.1 Datasets and Protocol 

We evaluate on four ETT benchmarks [1]: ETTh1/ETTh2 (hourly; 17,420 steps; 7 variates) and ETTm1/ETTm2 (15-minute; 69,680 steps; 7 variates). We use the stan-dard chronological splits established by Zhou et al. [1] and adopted by subsequent benchmarks [5–7]: ETTh datasets are split into 12/4/4 months (train/val/test = 8,640 /2,880 /2,880 steps); ETTm datasets are split into 12/4/4 months (train/val/test = 34 ,560 /11 ,520 /11 ,520 steps). Per-variate z-score normalisation is computed using 

only training-set statistics. Prediction horizons are H ∈ { 96 , 192 , 336 , 720 }; look-back window T = 336. All experiments for MSMixer , DLinear, and NLinear are run with three random seeds (42 , 2023 , 7) and we report mean results; standard deviations are provided in the ablation and sensitivity analyses (PyTorch [14], CPU training). 

5.2 Baselines 

We compare against seven baselines spanning both lightweight linear models and Transformer-based architectures: 

Reproduced baselines (trained under our protocol): 

• DLinear [7]: Moving-average decomposition into trend and seasonal components, each projected via a linear layer. 

• NLinear [7]: Last-value subtraction followed by a single linear projection, then adding back the subtracted value. 

Literature baselines (results from published papers, denoted †): 

• PatchTST [5]: Channel-independent patched Transformer ( T =336). 

• iTransformer [6]: Inverted Transformer with variate tokens. 

• TimesNet [9]: 2D temporal variation modelling via inception blocks. 

• FEDformer [4]: Frequency-enhanced decomposed Transformer. 

• Autoformer [3]: Auto-correlation decomposition Transformer. All reproduced models share the same training configuration (AdamW, lr= 10 −3,batch size 64, gradient clipping 1.0, max 15 epochs, early stopping with patience 3 on validation MSE). Literature results are cited from the original publications.

# 6 Discussion 

The experimental results reveal a nuanced picture of multi-scale mixing’s benefits. We analyse the key findings, their implications, and the limitations of our evaluation. 15 6.1 When Does Multi-Scale Mixing Help? 

The strongest improvements over DLinear appear on ETTm1 and ETTm2 (15-minute datasets), where MSMixer wins all 8 configurations with improvements of 1 .6– 9.7 % in MSE. On hourly ETTh datasets, MSMixer still wins 7 of 8 configurations against DLinear, with particularly large gains at longer horizons (ETTh1 H=720: 6.1 % improvement; ETTh2 H=720: 34 .2 % improvement due to DLinear’s anomalous degradation). This pattern has a natural interpretation: 15-minute data contains four times the temporal bandwidth per day compared with hourly data, creating richer multi-scale structure that the parallel branches can exploit. 

6.2 Competitive with Transformer Baselines 

A key finding is that MSMixer is competitive with PatchTST, the strongest Trans-former baseline, despite using ≈ 5× fewer parameters. On 4 of 16 configurations, 

MSMixer achieves the best MSE among all eight models (including one tie with PatchTST); on 5 additional configurations, it achieves second-best. The configura-tions where MSMixer matches or beats PatchTST (ETTh2 H=192, ETTm1 H=336, ETTm2 H=336, ETTm2 H=720) are all at medium-to-long horizons, suggesting that the multi-scale branch design is particularly effective when forecasting further into the future. 

6.3 The Role of the DLinear Shortcut 

The ablation study confirms that combining the scale branches with the DLin-ear shortcut (0 .385) outperforms either component in isolation: standalone DLinear (0 .393, +2 .1 %) or branches without the shortcut (0 .387, +0 .5 %). The fusion weight analysis provides additional insight: on ETTm1, the elevated α values (0 .57–0 .73) indicate that the multi-scale branches contribute substantially more than the DLinear shortcut, while on ETTh datasets, α ≈ 0.50 indicates balanced contributions. This dataset-dependent behaviour validates the learnable fusion mechanism. 

6.4 Scale Weight Distribution 

The learned scale weights show meaningful differentiation beyond the uniform initial-isation. The full-resolution branch ( w1) receives the highest weight on all datasets except ETTm2. ETTm1 shows the strongest departure from uniform, with the 16 ×

branch receiving only 26 % weight while the 1 × and 4 × branches each receive ≈ 37 %, aligning with the dataset where MSMixer shows the strongest improvements over PatchTST. 

6.5 Relationship to Classical Multiresolution Analysis 

The hierarchical scale structure of MSMixer bears a conceptual resemblance to clas-sical wavelet multiresolution analysis (MRA) [15]. In MRA, the signal is expressed as an orthogonal decomposition into a series of approximation and detail coefficients at progressively coarser scales. 16 The key distinction is computational: wavelet transforms use filters with specific regularity conditions and involve recursive 2 × sub-sampling, whereas MSMixer ’s average-pooling at arbitrary {1, 4, 16 } factors is a non-orthogonal, data-driven projec-tion. However, Proposition 1 establishes that complementary frequency coverage still holds under the average-pooling projection, providing the essential property needed for multi-scale representational completeness without enforcing strict orthogonality. 

6.6 Limitations 

Benchmark scope. All experiments are conducted on the four ETT datasets, which share the same domain (electricity transformer temperatures) and collection methodol-ogy. Evaluation on diverse domains (traffic, weather, exchange rates, energy) is needed to confirm the generality of the multi-scale mixing approach. 

Comparison methodology. We report literature baselines from their origi-nal publications, which may use different hyperparameters, training seeds, and data preprocessing details. The five Transformer baseline results are included for con-textualisation rather than strict head-to-head comparison. Our three reproduced models (MSMixer, DLinear, NLinear) share an identical training protocol and can be compared directly. 

Fixed pooling factors. The scales {1, 4, 16 } are chosen as round numbers that divide T = 336 evenly. For arbitrary T or datasets with dominant periods not aligned to powers of 4, custom scales selected by spectral pre-analysis may yield better results. 

Channel independence. MSMixer does not model inter-variate dependen-cies. On datasets with strong multivariate correlations (e.g. Solar-Energy, PEMS), a lightweight cross-variate module could improve results. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We proposed MSMixer , a lightweight multi-scale MLP architecture for long-term time series forecasting that combines scale-specific MLP branches at {1×, 4×, 16 ×} 

with a learnable softmax gate and a DLinear complementary shortcut. At 112 K parameters ( H=96) and O(T ) complexity, MSMixer achieves the lowest average MSE (0 .357) among lightweight models across all four ETT benchmarks with stan-dard chronological splits, outperforming DLinear (0 .386, −7.4 %) and NLinear (0 .365, 

−2.1 %), winning 12 of 16 configurations. Against five Transformer-based baselines from the literature, MSMixer achieves best or second-best MSE in 9 of 16 configurations while using 5 × fewer parame-ters than PatchTST. Notably, MSMixer outperforms PatchTST on ETTm1 H=336 (0 .367 vs. 0 .369), ETTm2 H=720 (0 .358 vs. 0 .362), and ETTh2 H=192 (0 .340 vs. 0.341), demonstrating that simple multi-scale MLP mixing can match or exceed attention-based architectures on specific benchmarks. Our theoretical analysis establishes that average pooling at different factors acts as a frequency separator (Proposition 1), that the softmax gate generalises single-scale MLPs (Proposition 2), and that the DLinear shortcut is necessary to recover full-window context lost during coarse down-sampling (Proposition 3). The learned fusion parameters provide interpretable evidence that both pathways contribute, with the 17 multi-scale branches dominating on high-frequency ETTm datasets ( α = 0 .57–0 .73) and balanced fusion on hourly ETTh datasets ( α ≈ 0.50). Future directions include: ( i) evaluation on diverse domains beyond ETT (traf-fic, weather, exchange rates) to test generality; ( ii ) data-driven scale selection via dominant-period estimation; ( iii ) cross-variate mixing for multivariate datasets; and (iv ) integration with larger foundation models as a lightweight multi-scale feature extractor.
