# ABSTRACT 

Masked diffusion models (MDMs) generate discrete sequences by iterative denoising under an absorbing masking process. In standard masked diffusion, if a token remains masked after a reverse update, the model discards its clean-state prediction for that position. Thus, still-masked positions must be repeatedly inferred from the mask token alone. This design choice limits cross-step refinement. To address this limitation, this paper proposes a simple, yet effective, post-training adaptation for MDMs that conditions each denoising step on the model’s own previous clean-state predictions. The resulting method, called Self-Conditioned Masked Diffusion Models (SCMDM), requires minimal architectural change, does not introduce a recurrent latent-state pathway, does not rely on an auxiliary reference model, and adds no extra denoiser evaluations during sampling. This is an important departure from partial self-conditioning approaches which requires expensive model training from scratch. In particular, the paper shows that partial self-conditioning, including the commonly used 50% dropout strategy for training self-conditioned models from scratch, is suboptimal in the post-training regime. Instead, once the model’s self-generated clean-state estimates become informative, the specialization to refinement is preferable to mixing conditional and unconditional objectives. SCMDM is evaluated across multiple domains, demonstrating consistent improvement over vanilla MDM baselines, achieving nearly a 50% reduction in generative perplexity on OWT-trained models (42.89 → 23.72), alongside strong improvements in discretized image synthesis quality, small molecular generation, and enhanced fidelity in genomic distribution modeling.

The core of SCMDM post-training is a two-pass mechanism that trains a single denoiser to first pro-duce an initial clean-state estimate and then refine that estimate under self-conditioning. In the first pass, the model predicts a clean-state distribution from the current masked input alone. In the second pass, this prediction is detached and passed back into the same network as a self-conditioning signal, and the loss is applied only to the resulting refined prediction. This allows SCMDM to adapt a pre-trained MDM toward iterative refinement without changing the underlying learning objective. A key contribution of this work is the identifi-cation of a distinct post-training regime for self-conditioning in masked diffusion models. While prior approaches often rely on stochastic uncon-ditional dropout (e.g., a 50/50 mixture of condi-tioned and unconditioned updates as established in [ 15 ]) to stabilize training from scratch, we find that this strategy becomes suboptimal during fine-tuning of converged backbones. Empirically, we show that once the model’s self-generated clean-state estimates are already informative, dropping the self-conditioning signal can be harmful, as it forces training to mix refinement-based updates with less informative unconditional ones. In contrast, finetuning with full self-conditioning consistently yields much stronger adaptation, suggesting this second pass refinement is preferable in the post-training regime. Empirically, we evaluate SCMDM across four diverse domains: natural language generation, molecular generation, genomic sequence generation, and discretized image generation. Across these settings, SCMDM consistently improves over the corresponding masked diffusion baseline with minimal additional compute. In natural language generation, SCMDM reduces generative perplexity on OpenWebText [ 16 ] from 42.89 to 23.72. On QM9, it improves molecular generation quality, including higher validity (594.2 ± 9.5 to 628.2 ± 15.0) and uniqueness (539.4 ± 12.6 to 546.6 ±

12.1). In genomic sequence modeling, SCMDM improves distribution fidelity across denoising-step budgets by up to 

10 .73% . For discretized image generation, SCMDM also improves the FID on CIFAR-10 by 9.12%. Figure 1 illustrates the empirical gains of SCMDM over the vanilla MDLM baseline across four distinct domains. Our proposed method consistently obtains performance improvements, even with minimal additional computation. The most substantial impact is observed in natural language generation, where SCMDM improves generative perplexity on OpenWebText by 44.69%. These results highlight SCMDM’s robust ability to refine discrete diffusion outputs, effectively bringing generated distributions closer to the target distribution. Together, these results show that simple self-conditioning can serve as an effective and broadly applicable post-training adaptation for existing masked diffusion models.

# 2 Related Work 

Discrete Diffusion Models. Building on the success of continuous diffusion models, denoising-based generative modeling has been extended to discrete state spaces for categorical generation with iterative refinement. Early foundational work by Austin et al. [17] formulated diffusion over discrete variables through structured forward corruption and learned reverse transitions, establishing a general framework for discrete diffusion. Meng et al. [18] introduced Concrete Score Matching , which provides a score-matching-style perspective for discrete distributions via continuous relaxations, and Lou et al. [19] showed that tractable density-ratio estimation can also yield effective discrete diffusion training. Building on these foundations, masked diffusion language models (MDLMs) [ 3] instantiate discrete diffusion with an absorbing-mask corruption process, yielding a practical and scalable non-autoregressive approach to sequence modeling. Recent large-scale models increasingly adopt this design for discrete sequence generation. For example, LLaDA [ 1 ] demonstrates that large-scale pretraining and instruction tuning can be carried out within a masked diffusion framework while achieving competitive performance with similarly sized autoregressive models. Related work has extended this paradigm to other domains, including code generation with DiffuCoder [ 2], molecular generation with GenMol [ 6 ], and fast language generation with Mercury [ 7]. Collectively, these works establish masked diffusion as a practical and increasingly competitive framework for modern discrete sequence modeling. However, standard absorbing-mask MDMs do not explicitly retain clean-state predictions for positions that remain masked after a reverse update. When such positions are still masked at the next denoising step, the model observes only the [MASK] token and must infer the clean token again, rather than refining its previous estimate. 

Self-Conditioning in Diffusion Models. Self-conditioning in diffusion models refers to conditioning the denoiser on its own earlier predictions along the denoising trajectory. For discrete data, Analog Bits [15] applies self-conditioning within a continuous-state bit-diffusion formulation, where discrete variables are represented as binary bits and diffused as real-valued “analog bits.” In text generation, Strudel et al. [14] study self-conditioning in a continuous embedding-space diffusion model by feeding back prior denoising predictions. These works demonstrate that reusing earlier denoising estimates can improve iterative generation, but they do not directly address post-training adaptation of pretrained absorbing-mask MDMs, where masked positions collapse to a single absorbing token and previous clean-state token distributions are not retained in the latent state. Several recent methods are closer to our setting because they also target cross-step information reuse in discrete diffusion. Loopholing Discrete Diffusion Models [ 12 ] addresses information loss by introducing a deterministic latent pathway that preserves pre-sampling information across denoising steps. This mechanism provides an explicit channel for carrying information beyond the sampled masked state, but it changes the model architecture and is not primarily designed as an efficient post-training retrofit for existing pretrained MDM backbones, instead requires training a new model from scratch with the additional deterministic pathway. Concurrent with our work, Residual Context Diffusion Language Models [ 13 ] recycle discarded token representations during diffusion decoding by injecting them back as contextual residuals at later steps. This approach also exploits cross-step information reuse, but it requires an additional reference model to construct contextual residuals during training, which increases training complexity and makes the method less appealing for use as a post-training adaptation. 

3Self-Conditioned Masked Diffusion Models 

Our work differs from these lines of research in three key ways. First , unlike [ 12 ] and [ 13 ], SCMDM is designed specifically as a post-training adaptation of existing pretrained MDMs, and thus it requires only minimal architectural modification and no auxiliary model . This is crucial as, differently from previous methods, SCMDM does not require training a new diffusion model from scratch, introducing a deterministic latent pathway, or constructing residual context with an auxiliary reference model. Second , it focuses on a specific failure mode of absorbing-mask MDMs: positions that remain masked retain only the [MASK] symbol, while the denoiser’s previous clean-state distribution for those positions is discarded. SCMDM carries this clean-state distribution forward and conditions the next denoising step on it, enabling still-masked positions to be refined across steps. Third , SCMDM incurs no additional denoiser evaluations at sampling time, since the clean-state prediction computed at step t + 1 is reused as the self-conditioning input at step t. This places SCMDM in a distinct post-training regime: while prior self-conditioning approaches often mix conditioned and unconditioned updates during training, we show that, once the base denoiser becomes sufficiently informative, full self-conditioning consistently outperforms the partial dropout strategy established in [ 15 ], revealing a refinement-specialization effect that has not previously been characterized. Our experiments further evaluate this adaptation across language, molecular, genomic, and discretized image generation, demonstrating that the method extends beyond natural language generation to multiple structured discrete domains. # 3 Preliminaries 

Let x = ( x1, . . . , x L) denote a length-L sequence over vocabulary V. For the diffusion process, each token xi is represented by its one-hot encoding over V. In natural language generation, V is a set of subword tokens (e.g., hello ,

cat ), whereas in biological sequence generation, such as molecular generation, it may be represented as SMILES [ 20 ], where V may consist of chemical symbols and syntax tokens (e.g., C, O). Discrete diffusion models define a Markov noising process which progressively noises an initial sequence x0 into a simple prior state xT . This time dependent corruption process x0 → xT is called the forward process and is parameterized by a set of transition probabilities 

q(xt | xt−1), for t = 1 , . . . , T . Following the forward noising process, a parametrized reverse process pθ (xt−1 | xt)

is learned to reverse the corruption xT → x0 and generate samples from the original data distribution. For masked diffusion models, the forward process is an absorbing corruption mechanism in which tokens are indepen-dently replaced with a special [MASK] symbol. The intermediate sequences xt for t = 1 , . . . , T are latent variables represented as a sequence of one-hot token states over V; when a position is masked, its token is set to the one-hot vector corresponding to [MASK] , which is denoted by M. Specifically, during sampling the model’s previous clean-state prediction is carried forward 

ˆx(t+1) 0 := xθ (xt+1 , t + 1) ,

The standard masked diffusion training objective is retained and applied to this second-pass self-conditioned prediction. Thus, SCMDM changes the denoiser input, but not the underlying learning objective. The complete computational procedures for SCMDM are formalized in Algorithms 1 and 2. Note that, while the training phase employs a two-pass forward mechanism to internalize the refinement objective, the inference procedure retains an identical amount of denoiser calls to standard MDMs by effectively reusing clean-state predictions from the preceding denoising steps. To maintain a consistent input interface for the denoiser during the two-pass training, the self-conditioning input for the first pass is initialized with the null tensor 0sc . This ensures that the first pass relies solely on the noisy input xt and the pre-trained weights, effectively serving as the model’s ’unbiased’ initial proposal before the refinement in the second pass. 

Sampling. At inference time, sampling is initialized from the fully masked sequence xT = ( M, . . . , M) and proceeds from timestep T down to 0. In this setting, self-conditioning incurs no additional forward passes: after ˆx(t+1) 0 is computed at step t + 1 , it is reused when evaluating the denoiser at step t, and the corresponding reverse kernel is then applied to obtain xt−1. At the first denoising step, no previous clean-state estimate is available, so the self-conditioning input is initialized with the null tensor 0sc . The full sampling process is illustrated in Algorithm 2. 

Integrating the self-conditioning signal. With the reverse process and the training and sampling procedures specified, the remaining design factor is how the self-conditioning estimate should be integrated into the denoiser. Two ways to inject the self-conditioning estimate into the denoiser are considered. In both cases, the goal is to integrate the representation of the current noisy input xt with a projected version of the clean-state estimate ˆx(t+1) 0 before passing the result through the backbone. 1. Concatenation-based fusion. The self-conditioning distribution is mapped from vocabulary space to the model hidden space via a linear projection 

proj sc : R|V| → RH ,

where |V| is the vocabulary size and H is the hidden dimension. The projected self-conditioning feature is then concatenated with the token embedding of xt and fused through a learned linear layer 

fuse proj : R2H → RH ,

yielding the combined representation passed to the backbone. 2. Additive embedding fusion. As a lighter alternative, the projected self-conditioning feature can be added directly to the token embedding: 

ht = embed( xt) + proj sc ( ˆ x(t+1) 0 ),

where embed( ·) denotes the token embedding layer. This treats self-conditioning as an auxiliary feature injected into the same latent space as the token embeddings while preserving dimensionality.

# 5 Experiments 

To empirically demonstrate the advantage of SCMDM as a lightweight post-training adaptation, we evaluate this method across four diverse domains: natural language modeling, molecular generation, genomic sequence generation, and discretized image generation. In each setting, we compare against the corresponding vanilla masked diffusion baseline and further examine both additive and concatenation-based self-conditioning variants, as well as full versus partial self-conditioning during post-training. These experiments test whether explicit reuse of prior clean-state predictions can reliably improve pretrained absorbing-mask MDMs, and whether the post-training regime favors full self-conditioning over mixed conditional/unconditional training. Notably, across all four domains, SCMDM consistently improves over the vanilla baseline.

The score distribution in Figure 4 shifts to the right under SCMDM, with the mean increasing from 33.1 to 36.5, indicating improved generation quality. Additional details, including the judge prompt and qualitative samples, are provided in Appendix A.1. A key result is that full self-conditioning is consistently more effective than partial self-conditioning in the post-training regime. While the rate = 0 .5 variant improves over vanilla MDLM, it remains substantially weaker than full self-conditioning across denoising budgets and evaluators. For example, under the GPT2 evaluator at 1000 steps, generative perplexity improves from 42.89 to 37.04 with rate = 0 .5, but to 23.72 with full self-conditioning. This supports our central claim that, once the base denoiser is already informative, dedicating capacity to unconditional updates becomes detrimental. Overall, these results show that SCMDM is an effective post-training adaptation for masked diffusion language models: it substantially improves unconditioned generation quality, zero-shot and test perplexity, and does so without increased autoregressive behavior, reduced token-level entropy, or major architectural complexity.

# 6 Conclusion 

This paper introduces Self-Conditioned Masked Diffusion Models (SCMDM), a lightweight post-training adaptation for masked diffusion models that conditions each denoising step on the model’s own previous clean-state predictions. Across natural language, molecular generation, and genomic sequence modeling, SCMDM consistently improves over vanilla masked diffusion baselines with only minimal architectural change. We further show that self-conditioning behaves differently in the post-training regime: once the base denoiser becomes sufficiently informative, full self-conditioning is more effective than partial self-conditioning. Overall, these results establish simple clean-state self-conditioning as an effective and broadly applicable post-training strategy for improving pretrained masked diffusion models.

Higher entropy means a more uniform token distribution and greater lexical diversity, whereas lower entropy indicates that probability mass is concentrated on fewer tokens, reflecting reduced diversity and potentially more repetitive samples. 

Autoregressiveness (AR-ness). To characterize how closely diffusion decoding resembles left-to-right autoregressive generation, we report both local and global autoregressiveness metrics as described in DiffuCoder [ 2]. Local AR-ness measures the tendency of the model to decode immediate successor positions in contiguous next-token order. Intuitively, Local AR-ness@1 captures how often the newly unmasked position directly continues a previously generated position, and higher values indicate stronger local left-to-right behavior. Global AR-ness measures the tendency to select one of the earliest remaining masked positions at each decoding step. In our experiments, we report Global AR-ness@4, which measures how often the newly unmasked position lies among the first four still-masked positions. Higher values therefore indicate a stronger preference for front-to-back filling and more autoregressive-like generation overall. 

LLM-Judge. For unconditioned natural language generation evaluation, in addition to the standard distribution matching metrics, we evaluate with LLM-as-a-Judge. The objective is to quantify the overall text quality and coherence 

of the generated text. This method is commonly used, and has been shown to generally match human evaluation performance [ 31 ]. For the results shown in Section 5.1, the Judge LLM used is Gemma 4 with 31 Billion parameters [23].

A.1.2 Biological Sequence and Image Generation 

We evaluate our method on biological sequence generation tasks using the Species10 and QM9 datasets; and image generation task with CIFAR-10 datasets. The detailed training and post-training hyperparameters are summarized in Table 9. For Species10, we train the model for 120K steps with a large context size of 32,768 and a batch size of 32, reflecting the long-range dependencies in genomic sequences. In contrast, QM9 involves shorter sequences, and we adopt a smaller context size of 32 with a larger batch size of 2048, training for 25K steps. For CIFAR-10, we train the model for 600K steps with a context size of 3072 (32 × 32 × 3). We use a learning rate of 2e−3 for Species10, 3e−4 for QM9, and 2e−4 for CIFAR-10 during training, with linear warmup over 3K, 1K, and 5K steps, respectively. In the post-training stage, we adopt a unified learning rate of 3e−4 for both datasets. All models are optimized using ADAM with (β1, β 2) = (0 .9, 0.999) , similar to [10]. For the genomics setting (Species10), we evaluate generation quality using Jensen–Shannon (JS) divergence over 

k-mer distributions, specifically 3-mer and 6-mer statistics. These metrics capture sequence-level patterns at different granularities: 3-mers reflect local nucleotide composition, while 6-mers encode longer-range dependencies and motif-level structure, providing a more comprehensive assessment of biological realism. For QM9, we adopt standard molecular generation metrics. Validity count the number of generated molecules that correspond to chemically valid structures. Uniqueness is computed as the fraction of distinct molecules among the valid set (i.e., removing duplicates within valid generations). Novelty is defined as the proportion of unique molecules that do not appear in the training dataset, reflecting the model’s ability to generate previously unseen compounds. For image generation, we use Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) to compare results, which is the standard and widely adopted measure for assessing sample quality in image generation tasks, providing a reliable estimate of both fidelity and diversity [32].
