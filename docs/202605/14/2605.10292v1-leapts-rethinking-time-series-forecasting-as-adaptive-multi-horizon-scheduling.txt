## Abstract 

Time series forecasting serves as an essential tool for many real-world applications, supporting tasks such as resource optimization and decision-making. Despite significant architectural advancements, most modern models still treat forecasting task as a fixed mapping from history to target horizons. This induces temporal decoupling across future time points and limits the model’s ability to adapt to the evolving context as forecasting progresses. In this work, we present LeapTS , a novel framework that reformulates time series forecasting as a dynamic scheduling process 

over the prediction horizon. Specifically, LeapTS organizes the forecasting process into multi-level decisions using: (1) the hierarchical controller to dynamically select the optimal prediction scale and advancement length at each step, and (2) 

continuous-time state evolution driven by neural controlled differential equations. Within this process, the controlled update mechanism explicitly couples the irregular temporal dynamics with discrete scheduling feedback. Extensive evaluations on both real-world and synthetic datasets demonstrate that LeapTS improves overall forecasting performance by at least 7.4% while achieving a 2.6× to 5.3× inference speedup over representative Transformer-based models. Furthermore, by explicitly tracing the scheduling trajectories, we reveal how the model autonomously adapts its forecasting behavior to capture non-stationary dynamics. 

## 1 Introduction 

Time series forecasting predicts future values from historical observations and plays an important role in many real-world systems, including energy planning [Hong et al., 2020], demand forecasting [Carbonneau et al., 2008], health monitoring [Gul and Catbas, 2009], resource scheduling [Hipel and McLeod, 1994], and strategic decision-making [Sezer et al., 2020]. In recent years, many advanced deep models have achieved strong performance by learning rich historical representations [Liu et al., 2024, Fei et al., 2025]. However, temporal patterns rarely evolve uniformly across the prediction horizon. Stable trends, abrupt fluctuations, and regime shifts can emerge at different future stages, making a fixed prediction process rigid and potentially brittle when confronting such dynamics. Such issue largely stems from the execution paradigms used in existing multi-horizon forecasting methods. As illustrated in Fig. 1(a-b), current methods mainly follow two paradigms. The recursive paradigm generates future values step by step with a fixed stride, feeding previous predictions back into the model and thereby preserving temporal ordering [Beck et al., 2024]. However, it suffers from severe error accumulation and computational overhead over long horizons [Marcellino et al., 2006]. As a result, recent deep architectures mostly adopt the direct paradigm , which predicts the entire horizon in a single forward pass from historical representations [Taieb et al., 2012]. While efficient, this design reduces forecasting to a fixed horizon-level mapping. This leads to three critical

Deep models increasingly rely on complex encoder designs to extract stronger historical representations, which may result in overfitting and reduce robustness [Wang et al., 2025c, Bergmeir, 2024]. Lack of contextual adaptability. Although temporal patterns may vary substantially across future stages, models cannot update their internal states based on intermediate feedback. This makes them insensitive to dynamic transitions during the forecasting process [Kim et al., 2021, Liu et al., 2022b]. Decoupled horizon dependencies. Future steps are naturally ordered, but the direct paradigm implicitly treats outputs as conditionally independent given the historical representation. This prevents models from capturing the temporal dependencies among them. Fundamentally, both paradigms suffer from structural rigidity: they enforce pre-defined execution paths that strictly decouple the forecasting process from the evolving temporal context. This raises a key question: 

How to adaptively adjust prediction behavior based on varying contextual dynamics? 

Motivated by these insights, we propose LeapTS , which reformulates forecasting as a coarse-to-fine dynamic scheduling process (Fig. 1(c)). Instead of using complex encoder-based architectures, LeapTS 

couples a global coarse predictor with an adaptive scheduling branch, and organizes the prediction process into multi-level scheduling to dynamically select the optimal prediction scale and step length. Furthermore, to keep the controller state updated as scheduling progresses, LeapTS incorporates two update mechanisms. The intra-step update mechanism compresses immediate forecasting results into feedback signals to guide the controller, while the neural controlled differential equation (NCDE) drives the inter-step state evolution to track continuous-time transitions between scheduling steps. With explicitly recorded scale choices, advancement lengths, and state updates, LeapTS 

provides a traceable scheduling path for analyzing dominant signals and scheduling granularity across volatility regimes. Together, these designs enable LeapTS to integrate adaptive step scheduling with feedback-driven state evolution within a unified framework. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: 

• We rethink the paradigm in time series forecasting and reformulate it as a dynamic scheduling process. This perspective shifts the focus from increasingly complex representation learning designs to adaptive prediction strategies that evolve with contextual information. 

• We propose LeapTS , a forecasting framework that dynamically adjusts prediction behavior according to evolving contextual information. Specifically, it incorporates a H-Controller , a hierarchical module for scale and step selection, together with a controlled state update mechanism to couple temporal dynamics, adaptive scheduling, and state evolution. 

• LeapTS provides valuable insights into prediction process through explicit scheduling mechanisms. We present visualization and analysis of the learned scheduling strategies, revealing how forecasting behaviors evolve during prediction and helping to alleviate the black-box nature of deep learning models. 22 Related Work 

2.1 Deep Forecasting Methods 

With the rapid evolution of deep learning, recent time series forecasting has been largely dominated by Transformer variants such as iTransformer [Liu et al., 2024], PhaseFormer [Niu et al., 2026], and EMAformer [Zhang et al., 2026]. These models rely on self-attention mechanisms to learn representations from historical observations and capture long-range temporal dependencies. However, although self-attention is highly effective in NLP tasks, its permutation-invariant nature makes it less suitable for modeling temporal order in time series forecasting [Zeng et al., 2023, Li et al., 2023]. Beyond Transformers, recent studies improve representation learning through diverse designs, including multi-scale modeling [Wang et al., 2025b, Hu et al., 2025], informative patching strategies [Wu et al., 2026, Liu et al., 2026], and frequency-domain decomposition [Fei et al., 2025, Yi et al., 2024]. Despite achieving strong performance, these approaches remain fundamentally representation-centric. They mainly focus on improving representation learning from historical observations, with forecasting implemented by directly mapping encoded representations to future predictions. 

2.2 Multi-Horizon Forecasting Paradigms 

The implementation of multi-horizon forecasting has evolved across distinct model families. Among them, the recursive paradigm is widely adopted by sequence modeling architectures. Classic autoregressive models like DeepAR [Salinas et al., 2020], as well as recent advanced state-space and recurrent networks such as Mamba [Gu and Dao, 2024] and xLSTM [Beck et al., 2024], fundamentally rely on this step-by-step generation design. However, this autoregressive formulation forces models to prioritize local transitions, often struggling to capture global structures over extended horizons [Zhou et al., 2021, Bergsma et al., 2023]. In contrast, the direct paradigm has become the standard for recent time series forecasting tasks. These methods employ fixed prediction heads to map encoded historical representations to all future horizons in a single forward pass [Wang et al., 2024b, Kim et al., 2025]. While effectively bypassing error accumulation, this projection reduces forecasting to a static multi-output regression and fails to explicitly model transitions between intermediate future states [Wang et al., 2025a, 2026]. Recent combination paradigms attempt to integrate the advantages of both strategies [Green et al., 2025]. Nevertheless, such pre-defined horizon partitioning imposes rigid constraints, which inherently restrict flexibility in adapting to diverse contextual dynamics. Different from all these paradigms, LeapTS is the first to reformulate multi-horizon forecasting as an adaptive scheduling process. This enables a uniquely context-aware dynamic prediction strategy.

3.2 Hierarchical Controller 

The H-Controller serves as the core module in the scheduling branch. Instead of generating the entire forecasting horizon at once, it decomposes the iterative forecasting process into a sequence of hierarchical scheduling steps. At each scheduling step k, the controller first determines which scale to use and then predicts how far the current step should advance .

The first is an intra-step summary-vector update within the H-Controller , which compresses the immediate scheduling outcomes. The second is the inter-step state evolution between consecutive scheduling steps, which propagates the scheduling state according to the current forecasting progress.

## 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we present LeapTS , a novel framework that reformulates time series prediction as a dynamic scheduling process. By integrating a hierarchical controller with continuous-time state evolution, LeapTS facilitates adaptive scheduling and effectively captures temporal dynamics. Extensive experiments on real-world and synthetic datasets demonstrate that LeapTS achieves superior forecasting accuracy and efficiency while exhibiting traceable scheduling behaviors. ## B Analysis for Multi-Horizon Forecasting Paradigms 

In this section, we provide a mathematical analysis of the upper bounds for three forecasting paradigms: the Direct Paradigm, the Recursive Paradigm [Taieb et al., 2012], and our LeapTS . Let ht ∈ H 

14 denote the underlying hidden state at time t. We define the optimal transition dynamics over a length of ℓ as ht+ℓ = f ∗(ht, ℓ ), and the learned forecasting model as ˆht+ℓ = fθ (ˆht, ℓ ). The forecasting error at step t + ℓ is measured by the distance et+ℓ = ∥ht+ℓ − ˆht+ℓ∥. To establish the theoretical bounds, we first introduce two assumptions: 

Assumption 1 (Lipschitz Continuous Dynamics). We assume the optimal transition function f ∗ is Lipschitz continuous [Asadi et al., 2018] with respect to the initial state. Specifically, there exists a 1-step Lipschitz constant λ ≥ 1 such that for any states h1, h2 ∈ H and length ℓ ≥ 1, the following holds: 

∥f ∗(h1, ℓ ) − f ∗(h2, ℓ )∥ ≤ λℓ∥h1 − h2∥. (21) 

Assumption 2 (Strictly Superadditive Approximation Error). We assume that for any state h ∈ H 

within the latent space, the local approximation error of the learned model fθ relative to the true dynamics [Janner et al., 2019] f ∗ is bounded by a strictly superadditive function ϵ(ℓ). Specifically, for any lengths ℓ1, ℓ 2 ≥ 1:

∀h ∈ H , ∥f ∗(h, ℓ ) − fθ (h, ℓ )∥ ≤ ϵ(ℓ), where ϵ(ℓ1 + ℓ2) > ϵ (ℓ1) + ϵ(ℓ2). (22) 

Naturally, ϵ(ℓ) is strictly superadditive, as predicting a longer horizon in a single forward pass forces the model to capture a significantly more complex joint distribution, causing the error to grow faster than linearly [Bengio et al., 2015]. 

Assumption 3 (Equivalent Residual Bounds). We assume the residual dynamics hres are bounded by the same Lipschitz constant λ and error bound ϵ(·) as the base dynamics.

## C Implementation Details 

Datasets Description. To comprehensively evaluate the proposed framework, experiments are conducted on both real-world and synthetic datasets, covering multivariate and univariate forecasting scenarios. As summarized in Table 7, the real-world datasets span multiple domains, including energy (ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1, ETTm2, Electricity), weather (Weather), finance (Exchange Rate, SP500), healthcare (ILI, COVID), web traffic (Wiki), and transportation (PEMS04, PEMS07). For univariate forecasting, the M4 dataset is adopted, which consists of large-scale heterogeneous time series across multiple temporal granularities ranging from hourly to yearly. In addition, we construct synthetic datasets to provide controlled evaluation settings, including two multivariate scenarios and one univariate scenario with consistent configurations. 

ETT [Zhou et al., 2021] covers transformer-related multivariate measurements collected from July 2016 to July 2018. The dataset includes variables associated with load conditions and oil temperature. ETTh1 and ETTh2 are organized at the hourly level, while ETTm1 and ETTm2 are recorded every 15 minutes. 

Weather [Wu et al., 2021] corresponds to meteorological observations collected throughout 2020. It contains 21 environmental variables, including temperature, humidity, and other atmospheric indicators. The data are sampled at 10-minute intervals.

The dataset consists of 321 variables, each corresponding to an 16 individual client. Exchange Rate [Wu et al., 2021] includes daily exchange-rate series over a long historical window from January 1990 to October 2010. Each variable corresponds to one currency-related series. 

ILI 2 records weekly counts of influenza-like illness patients from 2002 to 2021. 

SP500 records daily S&P 500 index data from January 1993 to February 2025. The dataset includes variables such as opening price, closing price, and trading volume. 

COVID [Chen et al., 2022] consists of daily COVID-19 hospitalization records in California during 2020. 

Wiki 3 records daily page-view counts for Wikipedia articles from 2018 to 2019. Each variable corresponds to one Wikipedia page, and the first 99 channels are used in this study. 

PEMS04 and PEMS07 [Liu et al., 2022a] are traffic datasets constructed from road-sensor measure-ments in California. The observations are aggregated at 5-minute intervals, forming high-frequency traffic flow series. ## D Length Ranges for Different Scales 

As introduced in Section 3.2, the H-Controller decomposes the segment length selection into a two-level hierarchical decision. Given the controller state hk at step k, the model first selects a temporal scale category c ∈ { short , mid , long } via a categorical routing module. Conditioned on the selected scale, a category-specific regression head then predicts a segment length within a predefined interval. This section details the construction of these scale-specific boundaries and their valid ranges. 

D.1 Scale Anchors

## E Synthetic Dataset Details 

Although real-world benchmarks evaluate overall forecasting accuracy, they often mix various complex factors, making it difficult to clearly assess a model’s specific architectural strengths. To explicitly isolate and test distinct behaviors under specific temporal complexities, we generate three synthetic datasets, each consisting of 20,000 time steps. 

Scenario 1: High-Dimensional Heterogeneous Series. This 30-dimensional dataset ( ∆t = 0 .05 )contains three latent clusters (10 variables each) to evaluate the decoupling of heterogeneous temporal patterns: 

• Cluster A (Continuous Periodic Patterns): Generated via the Van der Pol system [LaSalle, 1949] to simulate stable, non-linear seasonal fluctuations. dy 1/dt = y2 and 

dy 2/dt = μ(1 − y21 )y2 − y1 (μ = 2 .0), with observation noise N (0 , 0.05 2).

• Cluster B (Sudden Sharp Spikes): Generated via the FitzHugh-Nagumo model [FitzHugh, 1961] to simulate event-driven anomalies with sharp, rapid state changes. dv/dt =

v − v3/3 − w + I(t) and dw/dt = 0 .08( v + 0 .7 − 0.8w), where the intermittent pulse 

I(t) = 0 .5 if (t mod 50) < 2.0, and 0 otherwise. 

• Cluster C (Sharp Jumps with Slow Recovery): Generated via a damped harmonic oscillator [Strogatz, 2024] to simulate time series with decaying trends. dy 1/dt = y2 and 

dy 2/dt = −0.15 y2 − 1.0y1. The underlying states are periodically reset every 2,000 steps from U(−2, 2) to simulate repeated external shocks. 

Scenario 2: Causally Driven Multivariate Series. This 3-dimensional system ( X, Y, U , ∆t =0.05 ) simulates a multi-variable time series exhibiting severe non-stationary regime shifts. The core dynamics follow the classic Brusselator equations [Prigogine and Lefever, 1968], which are causally modulated by a slow-moving external driver U acting as an unobserved macro-trend. This exogenous driver smoothly tracks a periodic target: dU/dt = 0 .2( Utarget (t) − U ), where 

Utarget (t) = 1 .0 + 0 .5 sin(0 .1t). This hidden factor continuously drives the interacting variables (X, Y ): dX dt = 1 .0 + X2Y − (B(t) + 1) X, dY dt = B(t)X − X2Y (41) where the time-varying coefficient is dynamically governed by B(t) = 2 .5+2 .0U (t). By continuously modulating B(t), the unobserved driver forces the sequence to alternate unpredictably between flat, low-volatility baseline periods and explosive, high-frequency fluctuations. 19 Scenario 3: Univariate Series Driven by Hidden Variables. This dataset tests whether the model can forecast a 1D sequence when its underlying driving factors are unobserved ( ∆t = 0 .02 ). We generate the data using the classic Lorenz system [Lorenz, 2017], which produces complex, non-repeating patterns: 

dx dt = σ(y − x), dy dt = x(ρ − z) − y, dz dt = xy − βz (42) using standard parameters ( σ = 10 .0, ρ = 28 .0, β = 8 /3). To create a challenging forecasting task, we intentionally hide the interacting variables x and y. By keeping only the z sequence, we force the model to infer the missing hidden states and predict future values purely based on the historical movements of z.

## L Limitations and Future Work 

While LeapTS achieves strong performance, it still has certain limitations. Currently, our scheduling method is trained from scratch on individual datasets. Due to its limited model size, it mainly learns patterns within specific domains. To solve this, a promising future direction is to build a large-scale, universal scheduling model. By pre-training on massive and diverse time-series data, we aim to learn general scheduling rules. This would allow the model to perform zero-shot forecasting on various new scenarios without the need for retraining. 

## M Broader Impacts 

The development of LeapTS introduces several positive broader impacts for time series community and real-world applications: 

A Novel Paradigm for Time Series Forecasting. Our work presents a paradigm shift in time series forecasting from static mappings to dynamic scheduling. By continuously adjusting its forecasting strategies in response to the continuously updating context, the model can inherently adapt to evolving data distributions. This adaptive nature provides a robust foundation for achieving higher-precision predictions in highly non-stationary environments. 

Potential in Complex Systems. Beyond standard real-world benchmarks, our framework is particularly well-suited for modeling highly complex systems. In these scientific domains, where the underlying dynamics are often irregular and heavily influenced by unobserved factors, our dynamic scheduling mechanism offers a reliable and powerful tool for high-precision forecasting. 

Scheduling Traceability. LeapTS provides an explicit and traceable forecasting process. By revealing exactly how the model dynamically selects temporal scales and step lengths based on data volatility, it helps practitioners observe the internal scheduling dynamics.
