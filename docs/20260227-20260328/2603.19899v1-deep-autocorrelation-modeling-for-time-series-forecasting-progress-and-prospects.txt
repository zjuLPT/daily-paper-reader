# Deep Autocorrelation Modeling for Time-Series Forecasting: Progress and Prospects 

Hao Wang, Licheng Pan, Qingsong Wen, Jialin Yu, Zhichao Chen, Chunyuan Zheng, Xiaoxi Li, Zhixuan Chu, Chao Xu, Mingming Gong, Haoxuan Li, Yuan Lu, Zhouchen Lin Fellow, IEEE , Philip Torr, Yan Liu Fellow, IEEE 

✦

Abstract —Autocorrelation is a defining characteristic of time-series data, where each observation is statistically dependent on its predecessors. In the context of deep time-series forecasting, autocorrelation arises in both the input history and the label sequences, presenting two central research challenges: (1) designing neural architectures that model autocorrelation in history sequences, and (2) devising learning objectives that model autocorrelation in label sequences. Recent studies have made strides in tackling these challenges, but a systematic survey examining both aspects remains lacking. To bridge this gap, this paper provides a com-prehensive review of deep time-series forecasting from the perspective of autocorrelation modeling. In contrast to existing surveys, this work makes two distinctive contributions. First, it proposes a novel taxonomy that en-compasses recent literature on both model architectures and learning ob-jectives—whereas prior surveys neglect or inadequately discuss the latter aspect. Second, it offers a thorough analysis of the motivations, insights, and progression of the surveyed literature from a unified, autocorrelation-centric perspective, providing a holistic overview of the evolution of deep time-series forecasting. The full list of papers and resources is available at https://github.com/Master-PLC/Awesome-TSF-Papers. 

1 INTRODUCTION 

Time-series forecasting aims to predict future values based on historical observations, serving as a cornerstone for data-driven decision-making across diverse domains [ 1, 2]. In finance, it supports asset pricing and risk management [ 3]; in intelligent transportation, it enables traffic optimization and route planning [ 4, 5]; in the energy sector, it improves load balancing and grid stability [ 6]; and in manufacturing, it enables process monitoring and inferential sensing [ 7– 9]. With the rapid advancement of neural networks, deep time-series forecasting, which utilizes neural networks for forecasting, has emerged as a prominent paradigm [ 2, 10]. The scalability and nonlinear representational capacity of deep models enable them to capture complex temporal dependencies within large-scale datasets, demonstrating significant improvements in forecasting accuracy. Currently, deep time-series forecasting has become a foundational technique for handling the increasingly large, non-linear, and complex time-series data in modern applications [11, 12]. A fundamental characteristic of time-series is autocorre-lation [13, 14], where each observation is statistically depen-dent on its predecessors. For instance, daily temperatures are correlated with both recent observations and observations on the same date in previous years. This property distinguishes time-series from other data modalities, such as tabular data and images, and introduces unique challenges for forecasting tasks [ 15, 16]. From the early days of statistical forecasting, the significance of autocorrelation has long been recognized, driving the design of methods that explicitly incorporate it, such as ARIMA and VAR [ 17–20]. In the era of deep time-series forecasting, autocorrelation remains critical, as it exists inherently in both the input (history) and output (label) sequences used to train deep neural networks. Formally, this raises two research challenges: ❶ how to devise neural architectures to accommodate autocorrelation in the history sequence , and ❷ how to devise learning objectives to accommodate autocorrelation in the label sequence .

Addressing these two challenges has been a key driver of recent progress in deep time-series forecasting [ 15, 21]. To model history autocorrelation (challenge ❶), researchers have developed diverse neural architectures, such as recurrent neu-ral networks (RNNs) [ 22, 23], convolutional neural networks (CNNs) [ 24– 26], dense neural networks (DNNs) [ 27, 28], and Transformers [ 29, 30]. Recent years also witness notable inno-vations within each architectural family: state space models for RNNs [ 31, 32], large-kernel designs for CNNs [ 26, 33], deep decomposition techniques for DNNs [ 34 , 35], and large-language models (LLMs) for transformers [ 36]. These developments mitigate the limitations of earlier designs and substantially boost the capacity to model complex historical autocorrelation patterns. To model label autocorrelation (challenge ❷), researchers have developed various training objectives, grounded in likelihood estimation [ 15, 37], shape alignment [ 38], and distribution balancing [ 16, 39]. More recently, a new paradigm has emerged, which reframes forecasting as a conditional generation task [ 40], leveraging diffusion models [ 41, 42] or autoregressive objectives [ 43] to model label autocorrelation structures. Collectively, these two directions are complementary, with each addressing one of the two challenges inherent to autocorrelation of time-series. Despite rapid progress in addressing these challenges, a comprehensive survey that examines both neural archi-tectures and learning objectives through the unified lens of autocorrelation is noticeably absent. First, existing surveys predominantly focus on neural architectures, either detail-ing a specific architecture [ 44, 45] or broadly categorizing multiple architectures [ 1, 2, 46]. In contrast, developments in learning objectives have received comparatively limited

Second, prior reviews often emphasize methodological imple-mentations, without explicitly linking them to a fundamental underlying forecasting challenge. In particular, the role of autocorrelation—and the unique modeling challenges it creates—has not been sufficiently analyzed. This gap high-lights the need for a unified framework to understand the core challenges in time-series forecasting and the technical evolution of solutions addressing them. To bridge this gap, this paper presents a comprehensive review of deep time-series forecasting from the perspective of autocorrelation modeling, which makes two primary contributions. First, it proposes a novel taxonomy that integrates recent literature on both model architectures and learning objectives. Regarding model architectures, it innovatively covers emerging approaches—such as state space models [ 31, 32], mixture-of-experts models [ 49–51], large-kernel CNNs [ 26], and LLM-based models [ 52]—to help readers navigate fast-evolving trends; regarding learning objectives, it provides the first systematic analysis in this domain, which addresses a critical gap unexplored in prior surveys [ 2, 44, 45]. Second, it offers a thorough analysis of the motivations, insights, and progression of the surveyed literature from a unified, autocorrelation-centric perspec-tive. Section 3 details the proposed taxonomy and compares it with existing surveys. Section 4 reviews model architectures designed for modeling history autocorrelation, while Section 5 reviews learning objectives for modeling label autocorrelation. Finally, Section 6 discusses future research directions based on the further exploitation of autocorrelation. 

2 PRELIMINARIES 

2.1 Problem Definition 

In this section, we introduce the problem of multi-step time-series forecasting. We adopt standard notation: uppercase bold letters ( e.g., X) denote matrices, lowercase bold let-ters ( e.g., x) denote vectors, and lowercase normal letters (e.g., x) denote scalars. A time-series dataset consists of a sequence of chronologically ordered observations, denoted as 

S = {s1, s2, . . . , sN} ∈ RN×D, where N is the total number of observations and D denotes the number of variables (covariates) per observation [45]. At a given time step n, the time-series forecasting problem involves the following components: (1) the history sequence 

X = [ sn−H+1 , . . . , sn] ∈ RH×D, where H is the history length; (2) the label sequence Y = [ sn+1 , . . . , sn+T ] ∈ RT×D,where T is the forecast horizon; (3) the forecast model g, which maps X to a forecast sequence ˆY; (4) the model class G,which encompasses candidate architectures such as recurrent and convolutional neural networks. The task is to learn an optimal model g within the class G by optimizing an appropriate learning objective, such that for any given X, the forecast ˆY accurately approximates the ground-truth label sequence [21, 44]. Time-series forecasting tasks are generally categorized along two dimensions [ 53]. The first dimension concerns the 

forecast horizon : short-term forecasting targets relatively brief horizons (e.g., hours to weeks) with an emphasis on high predictive precision; whereas long-term forecasting addresses extended horizons (e.g., months to years) with a focus on capturing prolonged trends and seasonal patterns [ 15, 54]. The second dimension concerns the output format : determin-istic forecasting produces point estimates for future values; whereas probabilistic forecasting estimates the full predictive distribution for future values. The specific task formulation is driven by the typical requirements and constraints of the application domain [55, 56]. 

2.2 The Central Role of Autocorrelation 

Autocorrelation describes the statistical dependency between an observation in time-series and its predecessors [ 18]. This characteristic distinguishes time-series from the independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data commonly encoun-tered in other domains [ 57]. The degree of autocorrelation can be quantified using the autocorrelation function (ACF) [ 14], which measures the linear correlation between a time-series and its τ -lagged counterpart. Significant non-zero ACF val-ues at non-zero lags indicate the presence of autocorrelation.

Given its foundational role, autocorrelation is critical to ef-fective time-series forecasting. Classical statistical approaches have long recognized its importance [ 13, 14], establishing formal methods for its identification (e.g., Durbin-Watson test [ 58]), correction (e.g., differentiation [ 14], Cochrane-Orcutt transform [ 59]), and exploitation (e.g., ARIMA [ 18], VAR [ 17], and GLS [ 19]). In the context of deep learning, autocorrelation remains a central challenge, as it is inherent in both the input and label sequences. Consequently, two core research questions arise [ 15, 21]: ❶ how to devise neural architectures to accommodate autocorrelation in the history sequence , and ❷ how to devise learning objectives to accommodate autocorrelation in the label sequence .

3 TAXONOMY 

In this section, we present the taxonomy used to organize deep time-series forecasting methods. By way of preface, one key distinction warrants emphasis: this survey focuses on deep time-series forecasting methods that explicitly leverage autocorrelation. While topics such as denoised learning [ 172] and channel dependency [ 88, 173 , 174] are valuable, they are not unique challenges in time-series forecasting and fall outside the specific scope of this review. 

3.1 Taxonomy in This Survey 

In this subsection, we introduce the taxonomy centered on autocorrelation modeling to systematically organize the literature (Fig. 3). At the top level, the taxonomy divides the literature into two primary branches: model architectures 

and learning objectives . These branches correspond to the two challenges identified in the introduction: modeling history autocorrelation and label autocorrelation, respectively. Subsequent level offers a fine-grained classification based on the specific methods used to model autocorrelation. Within the model architecture branch, we distinguish be-tween non-Transformer and Transformer-based models. Ad-ditionally, we introduce plug-in modules designed to model history autocorrelation, which can be flexibly integrated into different architectures. Within the learning objective branch, we categorize approaches into likelihood estimation, shape alignment, distribution balancing, and conditional generation approachesEach category represents a distinct strategy for formulating objectives that accommodate label autocorrelation. 

3.2 Taxonomy in Related Surveys 

In this subsection, we review the taxonomies employed in existing surveys. Previous works have predominantly categorized the literature along three dimensions. The most common dimension concerns model architecture [ 2, 46, 53]. For example, Wang et al. [ 2] categorize models into DNNs, RNNs, CNNs, graph neural networks (GNNs), and Trans-formers. Along this line, several surveys focus exclusively on specific architectures, such as RNNs [ 175] or Transform-ers [ 44]. Another dimension concerns task formulation. For example, Benidis et al. [ 1] distinguish between deterministic and probabilistic forecasting. The third dimension concerns the application of general machine learning paradigms to forecasting, such as self-supervised learning [ 10], data augmentation [ 176], and diffusion models [ 177, 178]. In these cases, the taxonomy is derived from the technique itself; for example, Zhang et al. [ 10] categorize contrastive learning approaches based on contrastive learning approaches used. Notably, Jadon et al. [ 179] discuss common learning objec-tives, but the analysis does not recognize the challenge raised by label autocorrelation, focusing on general loss functions (e.g., mean squared error) rather than objectives specifically tailored to time-series forecasting.

4 MODEL ARCHITECTURE FOR AUTOCORRELATION 

In this section, we introduce model architectures for time-series forecasting. The central challenge is to effectively model history autocorrelation , i.e., the autocorrelation within the history sequence. Suboptimal modeling of history au-tocorrelation results in impoverished representations and degraded forecasting performance. Most current forecasting models adopt the direct forecast-ing (DF) paradigm [ 30, 76, 82], wherein a model g generates the entire T-step-ahead forecast sequence ˆY in a single forward pass. A typical DF architecture is formulated as: 

ˆY = g(X) = glinear ◦ gnn (X), (2) where a neural network encoder gnn extracts representations from the history sequence X, which are then projected by a linear layer glinear to produce ˆY ∈ RT×D. To model history autocorrelation effectively, various architectures have been proposed to implement gnn . We categorize these into non-Transformer models (Section 4.1) and Transformer-based models (Section 4.2). Additionally, we discuss plug-in mod-ules designed to enhance autocorrelation modeling, which can be integrated in different architectures (Section 4.3). 

4.1 Non-Transformer Models 

In this subsection, we investigate non-Transformer models, including recurrent neural networks (RNNs), convolutional neural networks (CNNs), and dense neural networks (DNNs). We highlight distinct rationales of each paradigm in model-ing history autocorrelation, alongside recent advancements such as state space models for RNNs [ 31], large-kernel de-signs for CNNs [ 26], and deep decomposition frameworks for DNNs [ 34]. These developments address the shortcomings of earlier designs and improve the modeling of complex historical autocorrelations.

> •

Limited Efficiency . The sequential update of zt precludes parallelization, leading to high latency for long sequences. To mitigate this, architectures such as linear recurrent units (LRU) [ 65], gated linear units (GLU) [ 71], and simple recurrent units (SRU) [ 70] have been proposed to linearize or simplify the recurrence, which enables parallelization and improves efficiency.  

> •

Memory Bottlenecks . The hidden state must memorize all history relevant for prediction, since predictions depend solely on the hidden state zt. However, the capacity of RNN is limited, which creates a bottleneck for modeling long-range autocorrelation. Early solutions such as long short-term memory (LSTM) [ 22] and gated recurrent units (GRU) [ 23] introduce memory states and employ gating mechanisms to selectively update them. For example, DeepAR [ 68] employs LSTM to encode history sequence. Another example is P-sLSTM, modernizing LSTM with an exponential gating mechanism [ 60]. However, these heuris-tic designs lack formal guarantees for retaining all relevant history. An alternative line of work uses polynomial-projection memories to compress history into a lossless, compact state [ 67, 184– 186]. This direction, pioneered by LMU [ 67] and formalized by HiPPO [ 184], frames memory construction as a function approximation problem, ideally retains all history relevant for prediction and provide a principled basis for subsequent improvements [185, 186]. Recently, deep SSMs have emerged as a promising solution to both challenges [ 63, 64, 66, 69]. A representative approach is Mamba [ 31], which enhances memory via structured state dynamics (HiPPO initialization [ 184]) and improves efficiency via a parallel-scan algorithm. These attributes have positioned Mamba as a versatile architecture in this field [ 32, 61, 62, 187]. For example, DST-Mamba [ 61] employs Mamba to model seasonal patterns in traffic predic-tion, and MixMamba [ 62] integrates Mamba with mixture-of-experts to improve non-stationary time-series forecasting. 

4.1.2 Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) 

The convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are originally designed for images to model correlations among pixels. They are adapted for time-series forecasting by treating sequences as one-dimensional images [ 76]. In this context, autocorrelation is modeled as the correlation between adja-cent time steps within a local receptive field. The popularity of CNNs is catalyzed by the temporal convolutional network (TCN) [ 24], which consists of cascaded causal convolutions with residual connections, demonstrating superior performance over traditional RNNs (e.g., LSTM [22], GRU [23]). A key challenge for CNNs in time-series forecasting is modeling long-term temporal dependencies, which is cru-cial for modeling high-order autocorrelation and achieving strong forecasting performance. This challenge can be inter-preted as expanding the effective receptive field (ERF) [ 188], defined as the range of input steps influencing the output. Current models expanding ERF can be differentiated by the operation domains as follows.  

> •

Time-domain models. They expand ERF by modifying the convolution operator. One line of work employs dilated convolutions (e.g., TCN [ 24] and WaveNet [ 80, 189]), which expand ERF exponentially without increasing parameters. Another line of work employs iterative downsampling 

strategies ( e.g., SCINet [ 77]), which partition inputs into sub-sequences and process them with separate convolution layers. This design expands ERF while losing information within each sub-sequence. To mitigate such information loss, DESCINet [ 78] introduces residual connections to enable intersection between sub-sequences; TimesNet [ 76] and MICN [ 75] perform multi-scale fusion to aggregate information from intermediate sampling layers. A more recent line of work expands ERF by employing excep-tionally large convolutional kernels , inspired by its success in vision tasks [ 33, 188]. This direction is exemplified by ModernTCN [ 26], which utilizes large-kernel depth-wise convolutions to approximate state-of-the-art performance 2. 

> •

Frequency-domain models. They expand ERF by operat-ing in the frequency domain. According to the convolu-tion theorem [ 190], a frequency-domain dot product ( i.e., 

frequency-domain filtering ) is equivalent to a time-domain depth-wise convolution with kernel size equal to the se-quence length. This principle underpins several frequency-domain architectures [ 25, 79]. For instance, FiLM [ 79] transforms the input to the frequency domain, applies a learnable frequency-domain filter, and then transforms it back to the time domain. FilterNet [ 25] enhances this by dynamically generating filters from the input, enhancing adaptivity and model capacity. Further advancements incorporate wavelet transforms to capture time-varying frequency characteristics, thereby improving robustness against non-stationarity [72–74].

They are adapted to time-series forecasting by treating the flattened history sequence as a feature vector. This direction is notably popularized by DLinear [ 27 ], which demonstrates that even a simple dense linear layer can effectively model autocorrelation and achieve competitive forecasting performance. Simplifying autocorrelation. This strategy transforms data so that simpler linear models become more suitable. > •

Enhancing architecture capacity. This strategy employs complex model architecture with improved capability to model complex autocorrelations. One approach in-volves replacing linear layers with multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) [ 86, 87]. For example, TiDE [ 87] incorporates a channel-independent MLP block with two hidden layers and residual connections; TSMixer [ 86] employs MLPs for both time-step and channel mixing. Furthermore, several works adopt multi-branch architectures [ 34, 35, 49]). For instance, TimeMixer [ 34] downsamples X into multi-scale subsequences and processes each with a dedicated MLP; MoLE [ 50] introduces a mixture-of-experts mechanism, which employ multiple linear expert models and an MLP-based router to adaptively weighs exprt outputs.

The self-attention mechanism models high-order auto-correlation structures by computing pairwise similarities across all time steps. However, a key limitation arises from the nature of time-series data: individual time steps often

4.2.1 Standard Self-attention Models 

The standard self-attention models often involve tokenizers to incorporate local context. As shown in Fig. 5, a typical tokenizer uses a sliding window to segment the series into overlapping patches, where each patch serves as a token that retains local context, which contains self-contained seman-tics and facilitates Transformers to model autocorrelation structures [ 100]. Since the introduction of PatchTST [ 29], tokenization has become a common component in many time-series Transformers [194, 196]. Building on the success of tokenizers, recent research has shifted toward adapting LLMs for time-series fore-casting [ 46, 196]. The key challenge lies in representation alignment —bridging the gap between time-series data and the textual representations on which LLMs are pretrained—an essential step for transferring the strong autocorrelation modeling capabilities of LLM to time-series forecasting task. Existing approaches can be grouped into four main paradigms based on their alignment strategies.  

> •

Query-based paradigm . It achieves alignment by con-verting time-series into textual sequences and reformu-lating forecasting as a question-answer task [ 101, 102]. A pioneer work PromptCast [ 102] offers an exemplar prompt template. This paradigm offers several advantages: it supports zero-shot forecasting, allows the incorporation of textual side information (e.g., task descriptions and dataset statistics), and can generate interpretable textual outputs. Subsequent works refine prompt templates to inject domain knowledge [197, 198], adapt text tokenizers to process continuous numerical values ( e.g., by sepa-rating integer and decimal components [ 101, 199]), and employ in-context learning to tailor forecasting to specific datasets [200].  

> •

Reprogramming-based paradigm . It achieves alignment by translating time-series tokens into the LLM’s word embeddings. A simple strategy is to use a linear projection layer to map tokens into the world embedding space [ 201]; during fine-tuning, only this layer is updated while the LLM remains frozen to avoid overfitting. Another strategy employs a cross-attention layer where time-series tokens 8

query a frozen textual embedding table to retrieve relevant word embeddings (e.g., Time-LLM [ 100], Time-FFM [ 99], CALF [ 202]). To reduce computation, the embedding table can be compressed into text prototypes via principal component analysis (PCA) [ 202] or learnable linear map-ping [ 100]. Contrastive learning has also been used to enhance alignment by maximizing similarity between time-series tokens and selected text prototypes [98, 202, 203].  

> •

Finetuning-based paradigm . It achieves alignment by finetuning LLM’s parameters on time-series data. GPT4TS [ 103] shows that tuning only the position em-beddings and layer normalization parameters can yield competitive results. This lightweight tuning strategy has been adopted in subsequent works such as GPT4MTS [ 92] and S 2IP-LLM [ 98]. Later studies incorporate low-rank adaptation (LoRA) to also update self-attention layers (e.g., LLM4TS [ 52], TEMPO [ 91], Time-LlaMA [ 204], MSFT [ 205]). These methods perform finetuning on a notably small subset of LLM parameters, which performs alignment effectively while mitigating overfitting on small datasets.  

> •

Pretraining-based paradigm . It achieves alignment by training foundation models from scratch using large-scale time-series corpora—comprising synthetic, real-world, or hybrid datasets [ 97]. Existing models mainly include 

encoder-only architectures trained with masked reconstruc-tion objectives (e.g., Moirai [ 93], UniTS [ 94 ], Moment [ 206]), and decoder-only architectures trained via next-step predic-tion (e.g., TimesFM [ 96], Timer [ 95], Time-MoE [ 51], Timer-XL [ 89]). Recently, Chronos [ 97] employs an encoder–decoder 

architecture trained with next-step prediction, also featured by innovative quantization-based tokenization and data augmentation strategies. Sundial [ 90] employs a decoder-only architecture trained with flow matching and releases a pretraining corpus containing over one trillion time points from diverse sources. 

4.2.2 Modified Attention Models 

The modified attention models refine the self-attention mechanism to incorporate local context. As illustrated in Fig. 5, a common strategy is inserting a transformation step before computing attention scores. Based on the operational domain, existing methods can be categorized as follows.  

> •

Time-domain transformations. These methods integrate local context through operations applied directly in the time domain. (i) Convolution-based transforms use learnable convolution layers to embed local context into query and key vectors [ 115, 117, 118]. For instance, LogTrans [ 117] utilizes causal convolutions for this purpose, demon-strating that convolutional processing helps attention scores identify relevant subsequences [ 115, 117, 118]. (ii) 

Downsampling-based transforms selectively downsample the attention matrix to enforce locality [ 114, 115]. Luo et al. [ 109] show that standard self-attention often pro-duces nearly uniform scores due to temporal redundancy, motivating downsampling mechanisms. Early methods rely on heuristic strategies, such as retaining neighbor-ing keys [ 114] or prioritizing queries with non-uniform attention distributions [ 115]. Recent works adopt data-driven downsampling; for example, DeformableTST [ 109] uses deformable attention to sample informative key steps; TimeBridge [ 105] uses an MLP to downsample adjacent queries. (iii) Transpose-based transforms transpose the input to capture inter-series rather than inter-step correlations in 

A [30, 110–112], treating individual series as tokens. In this way, linear transformations are applied per series to obtain query, key, and value matrices, thereby capturing local contexts. For example, AttentionMixer [ 111] advocates a two-stage attention mechanism that alternates between inter-series and inter-step attention for comprehensive representation modeling.  

> •

Frequency-domain transformations. These methods in-tegrate temporal context by transforming inputs into the frequency domain, where each componentent cap-sulates series-wise temporal context. For example, Aut-oformer [ 116] computes series-wise cross-correlations at different lags via efficient frequency-domain operations, using the results to aggregate lagged value vectors. FED-former [ 113] performs self-attention directly on frequency-domain components, yielding semantically rich attention scores. FreeFormer [ 106] further adds a sparsity penalty to the attention matrix of FEDFormer, reflecting the energy concentration property of frequency representations. Piao et al. [ 107] identify a low-frequency bias of Transform-ers, i.e., Transformers favor low-frequency features while neglecting high-frequency components. To address this, they propose Fredformer [ 107], which applies independent Transformer blocks to different spectral patches, yielding uniform modeling across frequency bands. 

4.3 Plugins 

In this subsection, we examine architectural plug-ins de-signed to enhance time-series forecasting models. Standard neural architectures often struggle to model time-series with complex autocorrelation structures, such as non-stationarity, coupled autocorrelations, and strong local dependencies. To address these challenges, several plug-in layers have been developed, which can be integrated into various backbone models with minimal modification. The three primary types are: (i) normalization layers to handle non-stationarity, (ii) decomposition layers to separate coupled autocorrelations, and (iii) tokenization layers to capture local dependencies. As illustrated in Fig. 6, these components are typically arranged in sequence: an input series is first normalized, then decomposed, and the resulting components are tokenized before being processed independently by the forecasting model. The individual forecasts are subsequently aggregated and denormalized to produce the final output. Since the discussed plug-ins concern no channel-wise interaction, they are described below using a univariate history sequence 

x = [ x1, x 2, . . . , x H] ∈ RH for clarity. 

4.3.1 Normalization

For example, a persistent upward trend leads to an increasing mean in time-series [ 207]. Such non-stationarity may introduce distributional shifts between training and test sets, which degrades model gener-alization and ultimately forecasting performance [124, 208]. 4.3.2 Decomposition 

Decomposition methods are employed to handle coupled autocorrelations (e.g., mixed trends and seasonalities) that can be challenging for a monolithic model to capture. They decompose an input series into several components, each exhibiting simpler autocorrelation structures, which are then modeled by dedicated sub-modules [ 210]. Existing decomposition approaches can be categorized based on the type of components they generate.  

> •

Seasonal-trend decomposition [113, 116, 125, 126]: it extracts a smooth trend component via a moving average, with the residual treated as the seasonal component: 

x(trend) = MovingAvg( x), x(season) = x − x(trend) ,

which is widespread in modern architectures [ 75, 91, 116, 125, 126]. For example, Autoformer [ 116] performs this de-composition within each block and focuses representation learning on the seasonal components; xPatch [ 125] per-forms this decomposition at the input stage and processes the two components using separate network branches.  

> •

Periodic decomposition [76, 127, 128]: it identifies domi-nant periods (e.g., daily, weekly) and decomposes the series into segments of corresponding lengths. For a candidate period Pe, the series is reshaped as: 

x(P e) = Fold( x), x(P e) ∈ R HPe ×Pe ,

where each row represents one period cycle. Dominant periods are typically derived from the most prominent frequencies in the Fourier spectrum of x [ 76, 127]. Times-Net [ 76], for example, decomposes the series into multiple periodic components and processes each independently with a shared Inception block.  

> •

Resolution decomposition [34, 35, 77, 78]: it generates multi-resolution components of the series by applying downsampling with different sampling factors Pr:

x(P r) = Downsample( x, Pr),

which is commonly used in multi-scale architectures [ 34, 35, 77, 78]. For example, TimeMixer [ 34] employs moving average pooling for downsampling, models each resolution component with a dedicated MLP, and aggregates the outputs for the final forecast. Tokenization layers aim to capture local temporal cor-relations that are not evident at individual time steps. Standard architectures, particularly Transformers which rely on token-level semantics, may struggle to learn meaningful representations from raw point-wise sequences. Tokenization addresses this by grouping neighboring points into tokens that explicitly encode short-range dependencies [126, 134].

5 LEARNING OBJECTIVE FOR AUTOCORRELATION 

In this section, we introduce learning objectives in time-series forecasting. The central challenge is to effectively model label autocorrelation , i.e., the autocorrelation within the label sequence. As a defining characteristic of time-series, neglecting label autocorrelation leads to suboptimal objectives and degraded forecasting performance. We for-mulate the objective for a univariate sequence ( y ∈ Y), as autocorrelation arises independently per channel and the formulation extends naturally to multivariate settings. Dilate [ 144] fuses SoftDTW with a temporal distortion index, 

i.e., the area between the optimal alignment matrix and the first diagonal, to quantify and reduce lag mismatches. STRIPE [ 140] builds upon Dilate for probabilistic forecasting to promote structured diversity in predictions. More inno-vations include using a Gumbel-Softmin operator for linear complexity [ 141], adding sparsity penalties to eliminate spu-rious alignments [ 146], and encouraging matches between steps with similar neighboring subseries [145]. 

5.3 Distribution Balancing 

In this subsection, we investigate learning objectives based on distribution balancing. These methods treat time-series forecasting as a conditional distribution alignment problem, where the learning objective is a discrepancy between the conditional distribution of the label sequence ( p(y | X)) and that of the forecast sequence ( p(ˆ y | X)): 

EDisc = Disc( p(y | X), p (ˆ y | X)) , (8) where Disc( ·, ·) measures distributional discrepancy. These approaches can avoid the autocorrelation bias inherent in likelihood estimation: minimizing EDisc to zero yields 

p(y | X) = p(ˆ y | X), which immediately implies that the model is perfectly trained regardless of label autocorrelation. However, designing a discrepancy that is both amenable to optimization and well-suited to time-series data remains challenging. Current methods differ primarily in how they define the discrepancy (see Fig. 9). 

5.3.1 Discrepancy Minimization 

Discrepancy minimization methods explicitly define a dis-crepancy Disc( ·, ·) and minimize it. An early approach, PS [ 48], defines this discrepancy using first and second-order moments and cross-correlation. While computation-ally efficient, using only low-order moments often fails to characterize complex label distributions, leading to residual misalignment. To overcome this, DistDF [ 16] employs the Wasserstein distance [ 219– 221], which can capture the full characteristics of distributions beyond simple moments [ 222– 224]; KMB-DF [ 148] proposes a discrepancy that compares all-order moments within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space, which achieves comprehensive distribution balancing. Generic statistical discrepancies may not fully accommo-date the specific characteristics of time series. For instance, the standard Wasserstein distance can struggle to capture temporal autocorrelations or adapt to non-stationarity [ 149]. This entails the design of tailored discrepancy metrics for time-series. The proximal spectrum Wasserstein (PSW) dis-crepancy [ 149] is one such solution, employing a frequency-domain pairwise distance to eliminate the impact of autocor-relations and incorporating a selective matching regularizer to improve robustness against non-stationary shifts.

5.4 Conditional Generation 

In this subsection, we investigate learning objectives based on conditional generation. These methods reframe time-series forecasting as a conditional generation problem: conditioned on history sequences to produce label sequences. Genera-tive models excel at capturing complex correlations within targets for realistic synthesis [ 228]. Conditional generation objectives leverage this property to produce forecasts that respect label autocorrelation, which may enhance forecasting performance when ample data is available. However, they also inherit the drawbacks of generative models, such as error propagation in autoregressive models and the high inference cost of diffusion models [ 40], which can affect their practical utility. Existing work mainly explores two generative models: diffusion models and autoregressive models in Fig. 10.

Architectural improvements of the score network. Tailored formulations of the diffusion process. The key idea is to customize the diffusion process to better suit time-series characteristics. MR-Diff [ 232] exploits the multi-resolution structure of time-series [ 166], extracting fine-to-coarse components in the forward pass and reconstructing coarse-to-fine details in the reverse pass, which offers physically meaningful supervision in each reverse step. TCDM [ 167] recognizes the autocorrelation structure in the label y and introduces temporally correlated noise in the forward process, which preserves autocorrelation structures in the latent state zτ as diffusion step τ increases. Recently, Sundial [ 90] demonstrates that conditional flow matching—a deterministic alternative to diffusion using ordinary differential equations—offers a more tractable training objective [ 233] while achieving state-of-the-art forecasting performance.

Autoregression is a prevalent paradigm for sequence gener-ation, spanning from traditional time-series models ( e.g., LSTNet [ 171] and DeepAR [ 68]) to modern LLMs [ 192]. Autoregression methods explicitly model label autocor-relation by conditioning each generated step on its prede-cessors. However, they face two key limitations. (i) error accumulation, where forecast errors made at early steps can propagate and amplify over the forecast horizon ( T); (ii) high inference cost, where the step-wise dependency prevents parallelization, resulting in inference costs that scale linearly with T. Both limitations can be catastrophized as forecast horizon increases. As a result, DF remains prevalent in modern time-series forecasting, as it avoids both critical limitations above, albeit overlooking label autocorrelation. To mitigate the above limitations, recent work has in-troduced patch-wise autoregression. Instead of generating a single step at a time, these methods produce a contigu-ous forecast patch (subsequence) at each step, striking a balance between fully autoregressive and fully parallel DF. Pioneering examples include Timer [ 95], Timer-XL [ 89], and TimeBase [ 168 ]. MoLA [ 169] further proposes a low-rank adaptation scheme to learn distinct representations for different forecast patches. This strategy is also widely used to adapt pretrained LLMs on time-series. In this context, time-series patches are learned to projected into the LLM’s token embedding space, and the model is fine-tuned to generate forecast patch autoregressively. Since LLMs are pretrained with an autoregressive objective, finetuning them in an autoregressive manner may better align the pretraining task, which can facilitate adaptation to forecasting [43, 170]. 

6 FUTURE WORKS 

In this section, we outline potential research directions to further advance time-series forecasting by improving auto-correlation modeling, in terms of both model architectures (Section 6.1) and learning objectives (Section 6.1.2). 

6.1 On the Development of Model Architectures 

6.1.1 Developing Lightweight Forecasting Models 

High autocorrelation implies significant information redun-dancy in time-series data, which complicates feature extrac-tion and often necessitates complex neural architectures. A promising strategy to reduce this complexity is to transform the data into a compact, decorrelated representation where autocorrelation is eliminated. For instance, OrthoTrans [ 54] employs PCA [ 234] to generate such decorrelated representa-tions. By removing autocorrelation from the history sequence, simple lightweight architectures—such as linear layers—may achieve low fitting errors with fewer parameters, collectively enhancing generalization performance. While effective, current decorrelation techniques rely heavily on PCA [ 234 ], which represents only one class of statistical transformations. Future work could explore a wider range of decorrelation methods and develop theoreti-cal guidelines for selecting optimal transformations across different forecasting scenarios. 

6.1.2 Developing Large-scale Forecasting Models 

Inspired by scaling laws observed in other domains [ 192], building large-scale foundation models for time-series fore-casting has attracted growing interest [ 89, 90, 95 ]. However, this direction faces two key challenges. First, a general-purpose corpus suitable for pretraining is lacking. Existing benchmarks (e.g., ETT [ 115], M4 [ 235]) are often domain-specific and exhibit heterogeneous temporal patterns, making it difficult for a single model to learn transferable representa-tions. Second, time-series tokenization remains insufficiently explored. As discussed in Section 4.3, raw observations in time-series carry limited semantics [ 30, 112 ], necessitating effective tokenization to provide semantic-rich inputs and unlock the potential of large-scale foundation models. Autocorrelation offers a unified perspective to address these challenges. ❶ Autocorrelation may facilitate the generation of high-quality synthetic data , which could help mitigate data scarcity. As detailed in Section 2, autocor-relation underpins canonical patterns like trends and season-ality. By defining the prototypical autocorrelation structures across different domains, we may generate synthetic data or augment existing datasets, helping models learn domain-invariant temporal dynamics. ❷ Autocorrelation may shape the design of tokenizers for time-series forecasting . While current patch-based methods [ 29, 125, 126] effectively encode local contexts, they may be less suited for scenarios with long-term, multi-scale, or non-stationary autocorrelation. Designing tokenizers that explicitly account for such complex autocorrelation patterns could improve the performance of forecasting models in real-world time-series datasets. 

6.2 On the Development of Learning Objectives 

6.2.1 Modernizing Canonical Statistical Methods 

The central role of autocorrelation in forecasting is firmly recognized in the statistical literature [ 13, 14]. Classical statistical methods often begin with a formal test for autocor-relation; upon detecting it, they either apply transformations to remove it (e.g., differencing [ 14] or the Cochrane–Orcutt procedure [ 59]) or build models that explicitly incorporate it [ 18– 20]. Exemplar models include generalized least squares (GLS) with correlated errors or Gaussian processes with structured covariance kernels. However, integrating these statistical principles into modern deep forecasting pipelines presents challenges. While offering valuable theoretical insights, classical methods like GLS and Gaussian processes are not designed for end-to-end neural network training. Their formulations often require access to the full dataset, which conflicts with the standard mini-batch training paradigm of deep learning. Consequently, a key research direction is to modernize these statistical concepts for developing learning objectives that explicitly account for complex label autocorrelations. 

6.2.2 Exploring Advanced Generative Methods 

Conditional generative models have gained interest for time-series forecasting due to their capacity to capture complex 15 

dependencies within target sequences [ 228]. This capability offers a promising avenue for developing learning objectives that excel label autocorrelation modeling, exemplified by [ 43, 169]. Future work in this area may focus on two complementary paths: ❶ Mitigating limitations of current generative models. Existing approaches inherit well-known drawbacks from their underlying generative models, such as the training instability of diffusion models and the error accumulation characteristic of autoregression models. These issues may be further exacerbated in time-series forecasting due to the typically long forecast horizons. Refining and adapting the generative models to alleviate these limitations is therefore a critical direction. ❷ Exploring advanced generative paradigms. Although the generative modeling landscape is diverse, most current forecasting methods rely on diffusion [ 40, 41] and autoregression models [ 43, 89]. Cutting-edge generative paradigms—such as bridge models, continuous normalizing flows, and neural optimal transport models—may enable beneficial inductive biases and enhance forecasting performance. For instance, Sundial [ 90] shows that conditional flow matching yields a more tractable training objective [ 233] compared with existing diffusion models while achieving competitive forecasting accuracy. 

7 CONCLUSION 

This paper presents a comprehensive survey of deep time-series forecasting through the lens of autocorrelation mod-eling. We first establish the central role of autocorrelation in forecasting and identify two core challenges: modeling autocorrelation in history sequences and modeling autocor-relation in label sequences. Afterwards, we systematically review recent advances in neural architectures and learning objectives designed to address these respective challenges. Building on this analysis, we outline potential future research directions to further tackle the two challenges and advance the field. In contrast to prior surveys, this work demonstrates that viewing both model architectures and learning objectives through an autocorrelation-centric lens clarifies the field’s evolution and open challenges.
